Aging is a complex biological process characterized by the progressive decline of physiological functions and increased susceptibility to disease, ultimately leading to death {Shaposhnikov, 2022 159}. Over the past several decades, research across diverse model organisms—from yeast and nematodes to flies and mammals—has revealed that the rate of aging is not fixed, but can be modulated by genetic, environmental, and pharmacological interventions {CITE}{Shaposhnikov, 2022 159}. Remarkably, many of the molecular pathways that regulate lifespan are highly conserved, suggesting that fundamental mechanisms of aging may be shared across the animal kingdom {Miller, 2020 240}{CITE}. 

While much of aging research has involved direct perturbation of these conserved pathways, a particularly intriguing dimension of aging biology is the discovery that sensory perception alone can modulate lifespan, independent of the physical acts associated with perceived stimuli {Pontillo, 2025 233}{Miller, 2020 240}. Studies in the nematode worm, \textit{Caenorhabitis elegans}, and the vinegar fly, \textit{Drosophila melanogaster}, have demonstrated that sensory cues related to food, mates, and even the presence of dead conspecifics can rapidly initiate physiological changes that influence patterns of aging {Chakraborty, 2019 25}{Gendron, 2014 83}[Sergei]. These effects are mediated by neuronal circuits that emanate from sensory tissues and interface with deeper regions of the central nervous system, ultimately engaging conserved neuromodulators—including biogenic amines and neuropeptides—that influence metabolism, stress resistance, and longevity {Miller, 2020 240}{Chakraborty, 2019 25}. 

Furthermore, motivational states \textit{per se} have been shown to modulate lifespan in \textit{Drosophila}. For example, the neuropeptide F system, homologous to mammalian neuropeptide Y, is a component of reward-, and stress-related processing, and its activation can reduce starvation resistance and shorten lifespan {Ryvkin, 2024 132}{Gendron, 2014 83}. Sensory perception of potential mates shortens lifespan in male flies through circuits involving NPF signaling, whereas successful mating reverses these effects {Gendron, 2014 83}, suggesting that an unresolved expectation of reward can influence lifespan. The motivational state of hunger also extends lifespan, despite driving behaviors normally associated with decreased lifespan\cite{weaver2023}. The observation that perception and motivational states can influence aging suggests that the nervous system does not merely relay environmental information, but actively interprets and integrates sensory inputs to generate internal states that prescribe physiological outcomes. Understanding the neural circuits through which perception modulates aging may therefore provide insight into how specific brain states impact health and physiology across taxa, including in humans.

\textit{Drosophila} provides a powerful system for dissecting these relationships, both because it shares conserved neuromodulatory and metabolic pathways relevant to aging and because its nervous system permits precise manipulation of defined neuronal populations {Miller, 2020 240}. Within the fly brain, the central complex (CX), a highly conserved region functionally analogous to the mammalian basal ganglia [strausfeld 2013], comprises the protocerebral bridge, fan-shaped body, ellipsoid body (EB), and noduli. This set of neuropils integrates multimodal sensory information and helps organize adaptive behavioral output {Buhl, 2021 22}{Xie, 2017 213}{Kahsai, 2011 37}. The ellipsoid body, so named for its toroidal shape, is subdivided into concentric domains innervated by distinct classes of ring neurons that process visual, thermal, mechanosensory, and circadian information {Buhl, 2021 22}{Kahsai, 2012 138}{Yan, 2023 115}[add more]. Recent work has implicated specific EB ring neuron classes in locomotor control, sleep, visual memory, and state-dependent action selection, suggesting that the EB is well positioned to link sensory representations to organismal state {Yan, 2023 115}{Kottler, 2019 266}.

Consistent with this, we have found that specific EB ring neurons contribute to lifespan regulation in response to sensory experience. Exposure of flies to dead conspecifics, termed “death perception,” reduces fat stores, lowers starvation resistance, and shortens lifespan in a manner that requires visual function and serotonin signaling through the 5-HT2A receptor {Chakraborty, 2019 25}. Furthermore, 5-HT2A-expressing R2 and R4 neurons in the EB are necessary for the lifespan effects of death perception, constituting a defined neural substrate through which environmental cues can influence aging {Gendron, 2023 128}. Insulin-like peptides, including DILP3 and DILP5, as well as the insulin-responsive transcription factor dFOXO, are required for aging effects of death perception, suggesting that EB ring neurons interface with conserved metabolic pathways implicated in lifespan control {Gendron, 2023 128}{Pontillo, 2025 233}. 

Despite these advances, fundamental questions remain about how EB ring neurons modulate aging. One possibility is that these neurons function primarily as passive conduits or gates for lifespan-altering sensory information: if their function is disrupted, flies may simply fail to perceive\cite{seelig2013} or internally represent salient stimuli, and phenotypes normally induced by those stimuli would not manifest. A more intriguing alternative is that the EB is an instructive generator of lifespan-modulating signals—that is, direct manipulation of ring neuron activity, independent of any external sensory input, would be sufficient to alter aging. More generally, the effects of EB neural activity on aging may depend on the internal states imposed by their manipulation.

Here, we use a variety of optogenetic tools, which enable temporally precise activation or inhibition of genetically-defined neuronal populations, to show that direct adult-specific manipulation of EB ring neuron activity is sufficient to extend lifespan in \textit{Drosophila}. Our findings indicate that these neurons can act as active regulators of pro-longevity physiology rather than only as conduits for perception-dependent aging signals. Activation of R2 neurons, inhibition of R4d neurons, and simultaneous activation of R2 and R4 neurons each extends lifespan in males and females, but these manipulations produce divergent transcriptional responses in head tissues, including distinct effects on insulin signaling, stress-response, and metabolic pathways. Despite these molecular differences, we detect no consistent changes in feeding, fat storage, locomotor activity, or climbing ability that account for the longevity effects, arguing against a simple explanation based on overt energetic or behavioral shifts. Valence assays further show that R2 activation alone produces a negatively-valenced state, whereas combined R2/R4 activation produces a positively-valenced state, indicating that distinct neural states with different immediate behavioral consequences can converge on increased lifespan. Together, these findings support a model in which the ellipsoid body serves as an integrative neural locus through which sensory and state-related processes can influence aging via multiple downstream physiological programs.
