\section{Introduction}
Aging is a complex biological process characterized by the progressive decline of physiological functions and increased susceptibility to disease, ultimately leading to death {Shaposhnikov, 2022 159}. Over the past several decades, research across diverse model organisms—from yeast and nematodes to flies and mammals—has revealed that the rate of aging is not fixed but can be modulated by genetic, environmental, and pharmacological interventions {Tain, 2020 27}{Shaposhnikov, 2022 159}. Remarkably, many of the molecular pathways that regulate lifespan are highly conserved, suggesting that fundamental mechanisms of aging may be shared across the animal kingdom {Miller, 2020 240}{Tain, 2020 27}. A particularly intriguing dimension of aging biology is the discovery that sensory perception alone can modulate lifespan, independent of the physical acts associated with perceived stimuli {Pontillo, 2025 233}{Miller, 2020 240}. Studies in the nematode worm, \textit{Caenorhabitis elegans}, and the vinegar fly, \textit{Drosophila melanogaster}, have demonstrated that sensory cues related to food, mates, and even the presence of dead conspecifics can rapidly initiate physiological changes that influence patterns of aging {Chakraborty, 2019 25}{Gendron, 2014 83}. These effects are mediated by neuronal circuits that emanate from sensory tissues and interface with deeper regions of the central nervous system, ultimately engaging conserved neuromodulators—including biogenic amines and neuropeptides—that influence metabolism, stress resistance, and longevity {Miller, 2020 240}{Chakraborty, 2019 25}. The observation that perception can influence aging suggests that the nervous system does not merely relay environmental information but actively interprets and integrates sensory inputs to generate internal states that prescribe physiological outcomes. Understanding the neural circuits through which perception modulates aging may therefore provide insight into how specific brain states impact health and physiology across taxa, including in humans.

\textit{Drosophila} provides a powerful system for dissecting these relationships, because its nervous system permits precise manipulation of defined neuronal populations while preserving conserved neuromodulatory and metabolic pathways relevant to aging {Miller, 2020 240}. Within the fly brain, the central complex is a highly conserved neuropil that integrates sensory information and contributes to the control of behavior {Buhl, 2021 22}{Xie, 2017 213}. Comprising the protocerebral bridge, fan-shaped body, ellipsoid body (EB), and noduli, the central complex receives multimodal input and helps organize adaptive behavioral output {Kahsai, 2011 37}{Thran, 2013 221}. The EB, a toroidal neuropil within the central complex, is subdivided into concentric domains innervated by distinct classes of ring neurons that process visual, thermal, mechanosensory, and circadian information {Buhl, 2021 22}{Kahsai, 2012 138}{Yan, 2023 115}. Recent work has implicated specific EB ring neuron classes in locomotor control, sleep, visual memory, and state-dependent action selection, suggesting that the EB is well positioned to link sensory representations to organismal state {Yan, 2023 115}{Kottler, 2019 266}.

This possibility has become particularly relevant with the observation that specific EB ring neurons contribute to lifespan regulation in response to sensory experience. Exposure of flies to dead conspecifics, termed death perception, reduces fat stores, lowers starvation resistance, and shortens lifespan in a manner that requires visual function and serotonin signaling through the 5-HT2A receptor {Chakraborty, 2019 25}{Gendron, 2023 128}. Furthermore, discrete populations of 5-HT2A-expressing R2 and R4 neurons in the EB are necessary for the lifespan effects of death perception, identifying a defined neural substrate through which environmental cues can influence aging {Gendron, 2023 128}. Insulin-like peptides, including \textit{dilp3} and \textit{dilp5}, as well as the insulin-responsive transcription factor \textit{Foxo}, are required for aging effects of death perception, suggesting that EB ring neurons interface with conserved metabolic pathways implicated in lifespan control {Gendron, 2023 128}{Pontillo, 2025 233}. 

Despite these advances, fundamental questions remain about how EB ring neurons modulate aging. One possibility is that these neurons function primarily as passive conduits or gates for lifespan-altering sensory information: if their function is disrupted, flies may simply fail to perceive or internally represent salient stimuli, and phenotypes normally induced by those stimuli would not manifest. A more intriguing alternative is that the EB is an instructive generator of lifespan-modulating signals—that is, direct manipulation of ring neuron activity, independent of any external sensory input, would be sufficient to alter aging. More generally, the effects of EB neural activity on aging may depend not only on which neurons are engaged, but also on the internal states they impose. In \textit{Drosophila}, motivational states associated with reward and aversion are encoded by distinct dopaminergic and neuropeptidergic circuits that shape behavior in response to appetitive and aversive cues {McCurdy, 2021 68}{Ryvkin, 2024 132}. The neuropeptide F system, homologous to mammalian neuropeptide Y, is a component of reward and stress-related processing, and its activation can reduce starvation resistance and lifespan {Ryvkin, 2024 132}{Gendron, 2014 83}. For example, sensory perception of potential mates shortens lifespan in male flies through circuits involving reward-associated NPF signaling, whereas successful mating reverses these effects {Gendron, 2014 83}. These observations raise the possibility that neural influences on aging are mediated not only by sensory detection or behavioral output, but also by the generation of distinct internal states that engage different downstream physiological programs.

Here, we use a variety of optogenetic tools, which enable temporally precise activation or inhibition of genetically defined neuronal populations using light, to show that direct adult-specific manipulation of EB ring neuron activity is sufficient to extend lifespan in \textit{Drosophila}, indicating that these neurons can act as active regulators of pro-longevity physiology rather than only as conduits for perception-dependent aging signals. Activation of R2 neurons, inhibition of R4d neurons, and simultaneous activation of R2 and R4 neurons each extend lifespan in males and females, but these manipulations produce divergent transcriptional responses in head tissues, including distinct effects on insulin signaling, stress-response, and metabolic pathways. Despite these molecular differences, we detect no consistent changes in feeding, fat storage, locomotor activity, or climbing ability that account for the longevity effects, arguing against a simple explanation based on overt energetic or behavioral shifts. Valence assays further show that R2 activation alone produces a negatively valenced state, whereas combined R2/R4 activation produces a positively valenced state, indicating that distinct neural states with different immediate behavioral consequences can converge on increased lifespan. Together, these findings support a model in which the ellipsoid body serves as an integrative neural locus through which sensory and state-related processes can influence aging via multiple downstream physiological programs.
