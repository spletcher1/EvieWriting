Aging is a complex biological process characterized by the progressive decline of physiological functions and increased susceptibility to disease, ultimately leading to death {Shaposhnikov, 2022 159}. Over the past several decades, research across diverse model organisms—from yeast and nematodes to flies and mammals—has revealed that the rate of aging is not fixed, but can be modulated by genetic, environmental, and pharmacological interventions {CITE}{Shaposhnikov, 2022 159}. Remarkably, many of the molecular pathways that regulate lifespan are highly conserved, suggesting that fundamental mechanisms of aging may be shared across the animal kingdom {Miller, 2020 240}{CITE}. 

While much of aging research has involved direct perturbation of these conserved pathways, a particularly intriguing dimension of aging biology is the discovery that sensory perception, and the motivations it creates, modulate lifespan independent of the physical acts associated with perceived stimuli {Pontillo, 2025 233}{Miller, 2020 240}. Studies in the nematode worm, \textit{Caenorhabitis elegans}, and the vinegar fly, \textit{Drosophila melanogaster}, have demonstrated that sensory cues related to food, mates, and even the presence of dead conspecifics can rapidly initiate physiological changes that influence patterns of aging {Chakraborty, 2019 25}{Gendron, 2014 83}[Sergei]\hl{cite Milli's paper too}. These effects are mediated by neural circuits that emanate from sensory neurons and that interface with integrative regions of the central nervous system, ultimately engaging conserved neuromodulators, including biogenic amines and neuropeptides, that influence metabolism, stress resistance, and longevity {Miller, 2020 240}{Chakraborty, 2019 25}. For example, in \textit{Drosophila} sensory perception of potential mates influences lifespan and stress resistance through reward-related circuits involving the neuropeptide NPF (homologous to mammalian NPY). These effects are reversed by successful mating {Gendron, 2014 83}, suggesting a role for mating drive and expectation. Similarly, the motivational state of hunger slows aging, despite promoting increased feeding, which is normally associated with decreased lifespan\cite{weaver2023}. These examples suggest that understanding how nervous systems interpret and integrate sensory information to influence conserved internal states will provide insight into how meaningful experiences impact health and physiology across taxa, including in humans.

\textit{Drosophila} provides a powerful system for dissecting these relationships {Miller, 2020 240}, and recent attention has been drawn toward the the central complex, which is a region of the fly brain that is functionally analogous to the mammalian basal ganglia [strausfeld 2013]. It comprises the protocerebral bridge, fan-shaped body, ellipsoid body (EB), and noduli. This set of neuropils integrates multimodal sensory information and helps organize adaptive behavioral output {Buhl, 2021 22}{Xie, 2017 213}{Kahsai, 2011 37}. The EB in particular, so named for its toroidal shape, is subdivided into concentric domains innervated by distinct classes of ring neurons that process visual, thermal, mechanosensory, and circadian information {Buhl, 2021 22}{Kahsai, 2012 138}{Yan, 2023 115}[add more]. Specific EB ring neuron classes have been implicated in locomotor control, sleep, visual memory, and state-dependent action selection, suggesting that the EB is well positioned to link sensory representations to organismal state {Yan, 2023 115}{Kottler, 2019 266}.

Recent work has demonstrated that the EB is an important component of the neuronal machinery through which sensory experience modulates aging. Visual perception of dead conspecifics, termed “death perception,” reduces fat stores, lowers starvation resistance, and shortens lifespan in a manner that requires serotonin signaling through the receptor 5-HT2A {Chakraborty, 2019 25}. Furthermore, 5-HT2A-expressing R2 and R4 neurons in the EB are necessary for the lifespan effects of death perception, constituting a defined neural substrate through which environmental cues can influence aging {Gendron, 2023 128}. Insulin-like peptides, including DILP3 and DILP5, as well as the insulin-responsive transcription factor dFOXO, are also required for aging effects of death perception, suggesting that EB ring neurons interface with conserved metabolic pathways implicated in lifespan control {Gendron, 2023 128}{Pontillo, 2025 233}. 

Despite these advances, fundamental uncertaintly remains about how EB ring neurons modulate aging. One possibility is that these neurons function primarily as passive conduits or gates for lifespan-altering sensory information: if their function is disrupted, flies may simply fail to perceive\cite{seelig2013} or internally represent salient stimuli, and phenotypes normally induced by those stimuli would not manifest. A more intriguing alternative is that the EB is an instructive modulator of aging-related processes, and direct manipulation of ring neuron activity, independent of any external sensory input, would be sufficient to alter aging. It is this latter case that, if true, suggests translational potential for targeting mechanisms of affective neuroscience \hl{REF} in aging research.

Here, we use a variety of optogenetic tools, which enable temporally precise activation or inhibition of genetically-defined neuronal populations, to show that direct adult-specific manipulation of EB ring neuron activity is sufficient to extend lifespan in \textit{Drosophila}.  Activation of R2 neurons, inhibition of R4d neurons, and simultaneous activation of R2 and R4 neurons each extends lifespan in males and females. These manipulations produce divergent transcriptional responses in head tissues, including distinct effects on insulin signaling, stress-response, and metabolic pathways. They also fail to produce consistent changes in feeding, fat storage, locomotor activity, or climbing ability that account for the longevity effects, arguing against a simple explanation based on overt energetic or behavioral shifts. Sophisticated behavioral assays revealed that R2 activation is negatively-valenced, whereas combined R2/R4 activation produces a positively-valenced state, indicating that distinct neural states with different immediate behavioral consequences can converge on increased lifespan. Together, these findings support a model in which the ellipsoid body acts as an active regulator of pro-longevity physiology and serves as an integrative neural locus through which sensory and state-related processes can influence aging via multiple downstream physiological programs.
