\section{Results}

\subsection*{Manipulating subsets of EB neurons extends lifespan}

Ellipsoid body ring neurons R2 and R4d are required for shortened lifespan in response to death perception,\cite{chakraborty2019,gendron2023} but how they modulate lifespan is poorly understood. One possibility is that they gate sensory information that would otherwise shorten or extend lifespan. R2 and R4d neurons respond to visual stimuli\cite{seelig2013} and transmit visual information into the ellipsoid body.\cite{omoto2017} If their function is disrupted and the fly can no longer internally represent or “see” a salient visual stimulus, phenotypes normally induced by perceiving the stimulus are unlikely to manifest. A second, more intriguing possibility is that, rather than acting as a passive gate or conduit of lifespan-altering signals, the ellipsoid body is a prescriptive modulator of aging. In this case, we would predict that directly manipulating activity in one or more neuronal groups comprising the EB would be sufficient to alter lifespan. To distinguish between these possibilities, we performed a series of experiments in which we manipulated the activity of R2, R4d, and R2/R4 neurons in the EB using optogenetic approaches.  

In dissecting the ability of different ring neuron populations to modulate aging, several considerations informed our experimental design. First, prior investigations of EB ring neuron effects on lifespan have relied primarily on constitutive, life-long manipulations, and we sought to eliminate potential developmental effects and to restrict treatment to adults with a fully developed central complex. Second, we wanted to avoid tools that require introducing confounding factors, such as high temperatures, which can significantly affect lifespan and obscure treatment effects on aging.[cite] Third, we wanted the flexibility to perform acute, cyclic, or chronic manipulations in ethologically-relevant contexts that involve lifespan, behavioral, and health assays. To accommodate these requirements, we decided to adopt an optogenetic\cite{govorunova2015} approach, allowing us to 1) restrict treatment to adults with a fully developed central complex;\cite{lovick2017} 2) oberve freely-moving flies under normal laboratory conditions; and 3) easily apply different treatment paradigms by changing light-activation parameters and exposure times. To assay lifespans, we housed flies in custom optogenetic rigs with programmable, interchangeable LED bars, accomodating different channel rhodopsins and allowing us to either activate\cite{klapoetke2014} or inhibit\cite{mohammad2017} targeted neuron populations. Flies were otherwise aged using standard husbandry conditions.\cite{linford2013} Unless otherwise noted, in all treatment paradigms flies were exposed to light-activation (40 Hz, 32 duty cycle) only during ZT 0-12 of a 12:12 light:dark cycle, beginning at 3 days post-eclosion and continuing through adulthood, promoting a normal circadian rhythm and restricting neuronal manipulations to periods when flies would typically be alert and interacting with their environment ({fig:lifespanfig}A).

We focused our investigation on R2 and R4, specifically R4d, neurons, as these are required for the perception of dead conspecifics to modulate lifespan\cite{chakraborty2019} and for food perception to enhance starvation resistance\cite{luo2021}. When flies expressing CsChrimson\cite{klapoetke2014} in R2 neurons (R2>Chr) were exposed to red light during subjective day, we observed a significantly extended lifespan in both males and females compared to siblings aged in the dark. This effect was not observed in genotype control flies that did not express CsChrimson, ruling out general effects of red light on lifespan and indicating a direct effect of neuronal activation {fig:lifespanfig}B). Activation of R4d neurons in a similar manner had no effect on lifespan [Supplemental]. However, when these neurons were silenced by exposing flies expressing the anoin channelrhodpsin GtACR1\cite{mohammad2017} to green light during subjective day, lifespan was significantly extended lifespan in males and females (\figref{fig:lifespanfig}C). These results should be interpreted with caution, because we observed that exposure to high-intensity green light caused a roughly 50\% (females) and 33\% (males) reduction in lifespan in all cohorts. Nevertheless, GtACR1 flies lived modestly but significantly longer than control flies in the light, and this difference was not observed in the dark.

Our results showing that R2 activation increased lifespan while R4d activation had no lifespan effect were somewhat unexpected, given that measures of R2 and R4 neuronal activity were observed to be higher after flies were exposed to dead conspecifics, which is known to accelerate aging and reduce lifespan in wild-type flies.\cite{chakraborty2019} We therefore decided to examine the effect of simultaneous activation of both R2 and R4 neurons using a split-GAL4 driver that targeted neurons in both populations. To our surprise, simultaneously activating both populations extended lifespan in males and females (\figref{fig:lifespanfig}D) to a \hl{greater} extent than R2 activation alone. While we observed that red light exposure during subjective day increased lifespan of CsChrimson-expressing flies as well as their genotypic controls in this experiment, the increase in lifespan was markedly greater in CsChrimson flies.

We observed that inhibition of the EB had no effect on lifespan, suggesting that reduced activity has no effect on aging in normal conditions. Simultaneous activation of \hl{most} EB neurons increased lifespan to an equal or lesser extent than R2 or R/R4 activation alone, indicating that R2 and R4 are the primary lifespan-modulating ring neurons and/or that the net contribution of increased activity in other ring neuron populations is to modestly shorten, rather than extend, lifespan.

Together, these data demonstrate that R2 and R4 neurons can be recruited to positively influence lifespan. Strikingly, manipulating even small sets of neurons—fewer than 10 per hemisphere\cite{omoto2018}—is sufficient to extend lifespan. Rather than merely gating or relaying lifespan-altering inputs generated upstream, the ellipsoid body appears to be an instructive generator of lifespan-modulating signals. Our results are consistent with known activity patterns of R2/R4 in other contexts. If optogenetic activation recruits R2/R4 in this feedforward signaling pattern, it may be that R2 activation, R2/R4 activation, and R4d inhibition are effectively the same intervention. Alternatively, they may engage partially or fully distinct mechanisms that converge on extended lifespan via different downstream mechanisms.

\subsection*{Different ring neuron manipulations produce distinct transcriptional profiles}

To investigate how R2 and R4 neurons modulate aging and whether they do so via similar or distinct mechanisms, we performed bulk RNA-sequencing on head tissues of flies after one week of R2 activation, R4d inhibition, or R2/R4 activation, under the same conditions used in the lifespan assays described above. We chose to sample flies after one week of optogenetic manipulation, to avoid survivorship bias that might arise at later time points while still allowing time for physiological differences to manifest. Flies were transferred directly from experimental rigs to liquid nitrogen, then decapitated by vortexing, and heads were collected with a sieve. Five biological replicates of 10 heads were collected for CsChrimson and control genotypes from flies maintained either in constant darkness or channel-activating red/green lights during the 12 hours of subjective day. Whole head samples comprised primarily brain tissue, although other tissues were present, including the head fat body and chitinous exoskeleton tissue. 

We used the DESeq2 package in R \cite{love2014} to first identify transcriptional changes associated with R2 neural activity. To accomplish this, we specified a model in which genotype (CsChrimson/GtACR1, control) and light stimulation (yes, no) served as main effects. A genotype x light interaction term represented changes specific to light-driven channelrhodopsin stimulation, and these were of primary interest to our analysis. One week after activation of R2 neurons, we identified 123 genes (70 up-regulated/53 down-regulated) whose expression values exhibited experiment-wise interaction p-values less than our cutoff of p < 0.01. Sample-based clustering using expression data from these genes produced clear separation, with the five replicates from each treatment forming four distinct groups [Fig xxx]. Hierarchical clustering revealed that samples from flies with activated R2 neurons formed a distinct group and that samples from CsChrimson flies maintained in constant darkness were more similar to samples from the control genotype than either was to the R2 activated treatment. Despite a stringent threshold of p < 0.01, we observed that one week of R2 neuron activation drove broad but pathway-enriched changes in the head. GO analysis of upregulated genes revealed enrichment for ribosome biogenesis and rRNA processing, insulin signaling, and response to starvation. Notably, these processes are not generally upregulated in tandem; decreases in IIS signaling have been shown to correspond to reduced translation in \textit{Drosophila}.\cite{essers2016} This suggests that direct R2 activation uncouples or differentially engages these two processes. Downregulated genes were enriched for protein folding and ER stress response pathways. Together, these results suggest that activation of R2 neurons induces a distinct \hl{metabolic state} in the head, characterized by altered nutrient signaling and reduced ER stress. It also caught our attention that many of the differentially expressed genes have been implicated in aging biology, with several known to modulate aging directly. For example, we observed the upregulation of multiple components of insulin-like signaling (e.g., \textit{foxo}, \textit{Thor}, \textit{InR}, \textit{dilp6}; upregulation of autophagy-related genes such as \textit{Atg101}; upregulation of genes involved in lipid metabolism, such as \textit{ImpL2}, which has also been linked to IIS-mediated lifespan extension [Partridge]; and higher levels of \textit{Cbs}, which encodes the rate-limiting enzyme in the transsulferation pathway and increases lifespan \cite{kabil2011}. Additional evidence for differentially expressed aging-related genes were identified at a significance cutoff of p < 0.05. These included upregulation of juvenile hormone esterase (\textit{Jhe}) and downregulation of genes involved in ER stress response, including UPR genes such as \textit{Hsc70}. Together, these results indicate that short-term, part-time activation (12L/12D) of a few dozen R2 neurons drives significant changes in multiple aging-, \hl{metabolic-}, and stress-response pathways.

We next asked how simultaneous activation of R2 and R4 compared to manipulations of the individual neuron populations. When R2 and R4 neurons were activated simultaneously, we once again observed excellent reproducibility with replicate samples within treatments being clustered together and flies with R2/R4 activated forming a cluster distinct from both genotype and light controls. In contrast to R2 activation alone, genes involved in starvation responses and lipid and carbohydrate catabolism were downregulated. The observation that multiple manipulations of EB ring neurons extend lifespan while producing divergent proximal outputs raised a fundamental question: what aspect of these circuit states is shared at the level relevant to aging? Activation of R2 neurons and co-activation of R2/R4 neurons produced distinct transcriptional responses without obvious common effects on feeding, locomotor activity, or overt physiology, making it unlikely that their pro-longevity effects arise from a simple change in behavioral state or energetic demand. Rather, these findings suggested that distinct patterns of EB activity may encode different internal states whose immediate consequences are separable, but whose downstream effects converge on lifespan regulation. In natural environments, ring neuron activity is recruited by stimuli that animals can behaviorally engage, including cues that promote approach toward nutritive resources \hl{REF} or avoidance of threatening conditions \hl{REF}. Because standard husbandry conditions are comparatively benign and homogeneous, however, the functional consequences of experimentally manipulating R2 and R4 neurons may be muted under baseline conditions. We therefore next asked whether these manipulations differ in valence, reasoning that their aversive or appetitive properties might provide insight into the functional logic through which EB circuits influence lifespan.

To determine whether lifespan-extending manipulations of EB ring neurons impose distinct internal states, we first examined their valence using the FLIC assay, which permits flies to associate optogenetic stimulation with self-initiated interactions at a feeding site \hl{REF}. In this paradigm, individual flies chose between a neutral water well and an attractive 10\% sucrose well, while closed-loop optogenetic stimulation (red light for CsChrimson or green light for GtACR1)was triggered selectively by contact with the sucrose well (Fig XX). To distinguish associative effects from non-associative consequences of light exposure, each “trigger” fly was paired with a yoked control that received identical light stimulation determined by the behavior of the trigger fly but without relationship to their feeding behavior. This design allowed us to ask whether activation or inhibition of specific EB neuron populations altered the motivational value of the sucrose-associated well while accounting for non-associative consequences of light exposure (increased hunger, for example). As with our behavioral assays, we performed these experiments both in light-naïve flies and in flies pre-exposed to light for one week or one month to explore whether chronic manipulation might induce neuronal or behavioral/physiological compensation. Because FLIC resolves several features of feeding behavior, it allowed us to assess the internal state associated with each manipulation across multiple behavioral dimensions. Each contact with the liquid was scored as an “interaction” (Fig. XX-Y), and these data were further parsed into the number of discrete well visits (“events,” Supp. XX) and the duration of those visits (“median duration,” Supp. XX). Increased sucrose interactions, more frequent visits, or longer visit durations were interpreted as evidence of positive valence, whereas rapid abandonment of the trigger well or learned avoidance was taken to indicate negative valence.  \hl{in the previous version there was reference to lots of figs in this text without discussion of results, yet. We first tested R2 activation, prompted by the observation that R2>Chr animals exhibited transcriptomic signatures consistent with stress and starvation-like state changes. If so, activation of R2 neurons might be expected to alter the motivational salience of a nutritive stimulus. Indeed, light-naïve R2>Chr trigger flies showed a marked reduction in interactions with the sucrose well relative to yoked controls, indicating that contact with the trigger well was strongly aversive despite the inherent attractiveness of sucrose (p < 0.01, Fig XX, left). This effect was accompanied by a significant reduction in event duration (p < 0.01, SupXX), suggesting that flies rapidly disengaged from the well once stimulation was triggered. Notably, however, R2>Chr flies did not reduce the number of visits to the sucrose well, implying that R2 activation did not simply condition avoidance of the well itself. Instead, these data suggest a more acute aversive state that promotes premature disengagement while leaving repeated approach behavior largely intact. This phenotype remained evident after one week (p = 0.035, Fig XX, middle) and one month (p < 0.01, Fig XX, right) of light pre-treatment. Such patterns were not observed in yoked controls \hl{FIG REF}, indicating that the negative valence associated with R2 activation is persistent and does not substantially diminish with chronic exposure.

Because the transcriptional profile induced by R2 activation suggested a starvation-like state, we considered whether the aversive effect of R2 stimulation might obscure a parallel increase in hunger. In principle, if R2 activation enhanced food drive while simultaneously imposing negative valence, then the immediate aversive response of trigger flies could mask increased sucrose consumption in animals that received identical stimulation without forming an association to the trigger well. To test this possibility, we modified the yoked design to included a single, wild-type trigger fly and two yoked animals, one R2>Chr and one genetic control. Under this configuration, both yoked animals experienced light delivery uncoupled from the food and at a frequency determined by normal feeding behavior. If R2 neuron activation increased hunger, we would expect the yoked R2>Chr flies, who in this case would not form a negative association with the sucrose well, to approach or interact with the sucrose well more often than yoked control animals who received equivalent bursts of light. Despite equivalent stimulation, however, yoked R2>Chr flies did not show increased sucrose consumption relative to yoked controls (p = XX), arguing against the idea that R2 activation induces a masked hunger phenotype.

We next attempted to assess the consequences of R4d inhibition. Interpretation of these experiments, however, was complicated by the green light required for GtACR1 activation. In our assay conditions, abrupt green-light delivery appeared to be intrinsically startling, producing reduced interactions and shorter event durations not only in R4d>GtACR1 flies but also in genetic controls. Although trigger flies expressing GtACR1 in R4d neurons displayed aversion to the sucrose well, the similar response observed in controls precluded a clear assignment of valence to R4d inhibition itself. Thus, under these conditions, the FLIC assay could not distinguish whether R4d inhibition is behaviorally neutral, positively valenced but obscured by the light-evoked startle response, or intrinsically aversive in a manner phenocopying that artifact.

Given these limitations, we turned to R4d activation as an alternative strategy to probe the behavioral state associated with this population. In light-naïve flies, R4d activation phenocopied key features of R2 activation: trigger flies exhibited fewer interactions with the sucrose well \hl{(p < 0.01, Fig XX)} and shorter visit durations \hl{(p < 0.01, Supp. XX)}, yet did not reduce the number of well visits \hl{(p = ??, Fig XX)}. Thus, as with R2 stimulation, R4d activation appeared to promote rapid disengagement from the trigger well rather than conditioned avoidance of the sucrose source itself. This aversive effect persisted after one week of pre-treatment \hl{(p < 0.01), Fig ?}, but was no longer evident after one month of chronic light exposure \hl{(p = ?), Fig ?}, suggesting that, unlike the response to R2 activation, the negative valence associated with R4d activation is subject to adaptation over time.

We then asked how flies respond when R2 and R4 neurons are activated simultaneously. Based on the aversive effects observed for R2 or R4d alone, it seemed reasonable to expect that combined activation would produce a similar or potentiated negative response. Instead, activation of R2/R4 increased both the number of interactions with the sucrose well (p < 0.01, Fig XX, left) and the frequency of visits (p < 0.01, SupXX), indicating that stimulation promoted engagement with the trigger-associated food source. These effects were observed in light-naïve flies and persisted after both one week and one month of pre-treatment \hl{is that true of both measures? Thus, combined R2/R4 activation did not increase the duration of individual feeding bouts, but rather increased the likelihood of repeated engagement with the sucrose well. These data indicate that simultaneous activation of R2/R4 produces a positively valenced state, in contrast to the aversive effects observed following activation of R2 or R4d alone.

\subsection*{Valence of R4d activation is uniquely context-dependent}



To determine whether the valence effects observed in the FLIC generalized beyond a feeding context, we next assayed place preference in a rectangular multi-lane arena in which one half of each lane was illuminated and the other remained dark. In contrast to the closed-loop FLIC paradigm, this assay used open-loop light delivery, allowing us to test whether the behavioral effects of EB manipulations reflected general valence rather than an interaction with feeding or sucrose-associated experience. Flies were monitored during an initial 10 min dark acclimation period, a 60 min treatment period in which the illuminated side cycled on and off, and a final dark recovery period. Preference index was used to quantify side bias, with positive values indicating preference for the illuminated side.

Consistent with the FLIC results, activation of R2 neurons in females produced a clear avoidance of the illuminated side during the treatment period \hl{(p = ??)}, and this place preference persisted into recovery \hl{(p = ??)}, indicating that the negative valence associated with R2 activation extends beyond feeding-related behavior and can support a conditioned place aversion. This effect was also observed under a higher-frequency neuronal stimulation regime, supporting the robustness of the phenotype across light conditions \hl{(Supp XX)}. Notably, the persistent avoidance seen in the arena differed from the FLIC phenotype, in which flies rapidly disengaged from the sucrose well but continued to approach it at normal frequency. This difference suggests that the behavioral expression of R2-dependent negative valence is shaped by assay context, potentially including the presence of food, the structure of the environment, or the temporal pattern of stimulation. In males \hl{(Little striking to be talking about individual sexes all of a sudden. Thus, the valence associated with R2 activation is sex-dependent in this assay, raising the possibility that the same circuit state may carry different behavioral significance in males and females.

We had been unable draw strong inferences about the valence of R4d inhibition in the FLIC because the abrupt light onset required for closed-loop GtACR1 activation appeared to be intrinsically startling. The open-loop arena reduced this concern, but the results remained difficult to interpet. In females, both controls and R4d>GtACR1 flies shifted toward the illuminated side during treatment, whereas in males both groups shifted toward the unlit side. These outcomes could reflect a sex-specific response to the light conditions themselves, or alternatively a manipulation-dependent valence obscured by coincidentally similar control behavior. Thus, although the arena avoided one limitation of the FLIC design, it did not fully resolve the behavioral consequences of R4d inhibition under the light regimes tested.

Activation of R4d neurons also yielded more context-dependent effects than were observed for R2. In the FLIC, R4d activation was aversive. Under similar conditions in the arena, however, female R4d>Chr flies showed little evidence of avoidance, maintaining a bias toward the illuminated side comparable to pre-treatment behavior and to controls. When light stimulation was presented at higher frequency, females instead shifted away from the illuminated side during treatment and recovery. Males exhibited avoidance of neuronal stimulation for all conditions \hl{true as written?}. Thus, unlike R2 activation, the valence of R4d activation in the arena depended on both sex and stimulation conditions, suggesting that R4d neurons may contribute to behavioral state in a more context-sensitive or modulatory manner.

Finally, we asked whether the appetitive effect of simultaneous R2/R4 activation observed in the FLIC would generalize across contexts. In contrast to the variable effects of R4d alone, co-activation of R2 and R4 produced a strong preference for the illuminated side in both females and males, and this preference persisted into the recovery period. Preference was robust to different neuronal stimulation regimes. Thus, the positive valence associated with R2/R4 activation was robust across behavioral assays, light conditions, and sex. 

Given that R4d activation produced different results in the arena than in the FLIC, we wondered whether the strong positive valence of R2/R4 activation would hold across contexts, like R2 activation, or whether it would track R4d’s context dependence. When simultaneously activating R2 and R4 neurons at 48 Hz, we observed a strong preference for the lit side of the arena in both females (Fig XX) and males (Fig XX) that persisted during the recovery phase. This strong light preference was also observed at 200 Hz for both sexes (Supp XX). The positive valence of simultaneous R2/R4 activation was therefore robust across different light conditions and behavioral assays, producing behaviors distinct from activation of R2 and R4d alone. 

In summary, the valence experiments reveal that EB-dependent regulation of behavior is nonlinear and depends on both circuit composition and behavioral context. Across two complementary assays, activation of R2 alone consistently produced negative valence in females, whereas co-activation of R2/R4 produced a robust positive valence that generalized across feeding-dependent and feeding-independent paradigms and persisted after chronic light exposure, indicating that these effects reflect stable properties of EB circuit state rather than acute stimulation artifacts. In the FLIC, the R2/R4 phenotype was characterized by increased interactions and more frequent returns without prolonged visit duration, a pattern consistent with enhanced approach or incentive salience rather than simply increased consummatory behavior per se. By contrast, the behavioral consequences of R4d manipulation were more context dependent, and the place-preference experiments further revealed that the valence associated with R2 activation can differ by sex, suggesting that the same circuit output may be interpreted differently depending on internal state or life-history context. Together, these findings support a model in which EB ring neurons do not make fixed, additive contributions to motivational state, but instead generate qualitatively distinct internal states through combinatorial recruitment. This raises the intriguing possibility that distinct EB activity states engage different downstream programs while nevertheless converging on lifespan regulation.
