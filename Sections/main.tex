\section{Introduction}
Aging is a complex biological process characterized by the progressive decline of physiological functions and increased susceptibility to disease, ultimately leading to death {Shaposhnikov, 2022 #159}. Over the past several decades, research across diverse model organisms—from yeast and nematodes to flies and mammals—has revealed that the rate of aging is not fixed but can be modulated by genetic, environmental, and pharmacological interventions {Tain, 2020 #27}{Shaposhnikov, 2022 #159}. Remarkably, many of the molecular pathways that regulate lifespan are highly conserved, suggesting that fundamental mechanisms of aging may be shared across the animal kingdom {Miller, 2020 #240}{Tain, 2020 #27}. A particularly intriguing dimension of aging biology is the discovery that sensory perception alone can modulate lifespan, independent of the physical acts associated with perceived stimuli {Pontillo, 2025 #233}{Miller, 2020 #240}. Studies in the nematode worm, \textit{Caenorhabitis elegans}, and the vinegar fly, \textit{Drosophila melanogaster}, have demonstrated that sensory cues related to food, mates, and even the presence of dead conspecifics can rapidly initiate physiological changes that influence patterns of aging {Chakraborty, 2019 #25}{Gendron, 2014 #83}. These effects are mediated by neuronal circuits that emanate from sensory tissues and interface with deeper regions of the central nervous system, ultimately engaging conserved neuromodulators—including biogenic amines and neuropeptides—that influence metabolism, stress resistance, and longevity {Miller, 2020 #240}{Chakraborty, 2019 #25}. The observation that perception can influence aging suggests that the nervous system does not merely relay environmental information but actively interprets and integrates sensory inputs to generate internal states that prescribe physiological outcomes. Understanding the neural circuits through which perception modulates aging may therefore provide insight into how specific brain states impact health and physiology across taxa, including in humans.

\textit{Drosophila} provides a powerful system for dissecting these relationships, because its nervous system permits precise manipulation of defined neuronal populations while preserving conserved neuromodulatory and metabolic pathways relevant to aging {Miller, 2020 #240}. Within the fly brain, the central complex is a highly conserved neuropil that integrates sensory information and contributes to the control of behavior {Buhl, 2021 #22}{Xie, 2017 #213}. Comprising the protocerebral bridge, fan-shaped body, ellipsoid body (EB), and noduli, the central complex receives multimodal input and helps organize adaptive behavioral output {Kahsai, 2011 #37}{Thran, 2013 #221}. The EB, a toroidal neuropil within the central complex, is subdivided into concentric domains innervated by distinct classes of ring neurons that process visual, thermal, mechanosensory, and circadian information {Buhl, 2021 #22}{Kahsai, 2012 #138}{Yan, 2023 #115}. Recent work has implicated specific EB ring neuron classes in locomotor control, sleep, visual memory, and state-dependent action selection, suggesting that the EB is well positioned to link sensory representations to organismal state {Yan, 2023 #115}{Kottler, 2019 #266}.

This possibility has become particularly relevant with the observation that specific EB ring neurons contribute to lifespan regulation in response to sensory experience. Exposure of flies to dead conspecifics, termed death perception, reduces fat stores, lowers starvation resistance, and shortens lifespan in a manner that requires visual function and serotonin signaling through the 5-HT2A receptor {Chakraborty, 2019 #25}{Gendron, 2023 #128}. Furthermore, discrete populations of 5-HT2A-expressing R2 and R4 neurons in the EB are necessary for the lifespan effects of death perception, identifying a defined neural substrate through which environmental cues can influence aging {Gendron, 2023 #128}. Insulin-like peptides, including \textit{dilp3} and \textit{dilp5}, as well as the insulin-responsive transcription factor \textit{Foxo}, are required for aging effects of death perception, suggesting that EB ring neurons interface with conserved metabolic pathways implicated in lifespan control {Gendron, 2023 #128}{Pontillo, 2025 #233}. 

Despite these advances, fundamental questions remain about how EB ring neurons modulate aging. One possibility is that these neurons function primarily as passive conduits or gates for lifespan-altering sensory information: if their function is disrupted, flies may simply fail to perceive or internally represent salient stimuli, and phenotypes normally induced by those stimuli would not manifest. A more intriguing alternative is that the EB is an instructive generator of lifespan-modulating signals—that is, direct manipulation of ring neuron activity, independent of any external sensory input, would be sufficient to alter aging. More generally, the effects of EB neural activity on aging may depend not only on which neurons are engaged, but also on the internal states they impose. In \textit{Drosophila}, motivational states associated with reward and aversion are encoded by distinct dopaminergic and neuropeptidergic circuits that shape behavior in response to appetitive and aversive cues {McCurdy, 2021 #68}{Ryvkin, 2024 #132}. The neuropeptide F system, homologous to mammalian neuropeptide Y, is a component of reward and stress-related processing, and its activation can reduce starvation resistance and lifespan {Ryvkin, 2024 #132}{Gendron, 2014 #83}. For example, sensory perception of potential mates shortens lifespan in male flies through circuits involving reward-associated NPF signaling, whereas successful mating reverses these effects {Gendron, 2014 #83}. These observations raise the possibility that neural influences on aging are mediated not only by sensory detection or behavioral output, but also by the generation of distinct internal states that engage different downstream physiological programs.

Here, we use a variety of optogenetic tools, which enable temporally precise activation or inhibition of genetically defined neuronal populations using light, to show that direct adult-specific manipulation of EB ring neuron activity is sufficient to extend lifespan in \textit{Drosophila}, indicating that these neurons can act as active regulators of pro-longevity physiology rather than only as conduits for perception-dependent aging signals. Activation of R2 neurons, inhibition of R4d neurons, and simultaneous activation of R2 and R4 neurons each extend lifespan in males and females, but these manipulations produce divergent transcriptional responses in head tissues, including distinct effects on insulin signaling, stress-response, and metabolic pathways. Despite these molecular differences, we detect no consistent changes in feeding, fat storage, locomotor activity, or climbing ability that account for the longevity effects, arguing against a simple explanation based on overt energetic or behavioral shifts. Valence assays further show that R2 activation alone produces a negatively valenced state, whereas combined R2/R4 activation produces a positively valenced state, indicating that distinct neural states with different immediate behavioral consequences can converge on increased lifespan. Together, these findings support a model in which the ellipsoid body serves as an integrative neural locus through which sensory and state-related processes can influence aging via multiple downstream physiological programs.

\subsection*{Manipulating subsets of EB neurons extends lifespan}

Ellipsoid body ring neurons R2 and R4d are required for shortened lifespan in response to death perception,\cite{chakraborty2019,gendron2023} but how they modulate lifespan is poorly understood. One possibility is that they gate sensory information that would otherwise shorten or extend lifespan. R2 and R4d neurons respond to visual stimuli\cite{seelig2013} and transmit visual information into the ellipsoid body.\cite{omoto2017} If their function is disrupted and the fly can no longer internally represent or “see” a salient visual stimulus, phenotypes normally induced by perceiving the stimulus are unlikely to manifest. A second, more intriguing possibility is that, rather than acting as a passive gate or conduit of lifespan-altering signals, the ellipsoid body is a prescriptive modulator of aging. In this case, we would predict that directly manipulating activity in one or more neuronal groups comprising the EB would be sufficient to alter lifespan. To distinguish between these possibilities, we performed a series of experiments in which we manipulated the activity of R2, R4d, and R2/R4 neurons in the EB using optogenetic approaches.  

In dissecting the ability of different ring neuron populations to modulate aging, several considerations informed our experimental design. First, prior investigations of EB ring neuron effects on lifespan have relied primarily on constitutive, life-long manipulations, and we sought to eliminate potential developmental effects and to restrict treatment to adults with a fully developed central complex. Second, we wanted to avoid tools that require introducing confounding factors, such as high temperatures, which can significantly affect lifespan and obscure treatment effects on aging.[cite] Third, we wanted the flexibility to perform acute, cyclic, or chronic manipulations in ethologically-relevant contexts that involve lifespan, behavioral, and health assays. To accommodate these requirements, we decided to adopt an optogenetic\cite{govorunova2015} approach, allowing us to 1) restrict treatment to adults with a fully developed central complex;\cite{lovick2017} 2) oberve freely-moving flies under normal laboratory conditions; and 3) easily apply different treatment paradigms by changing light-activation parameters and exposure times. To assay lifespans, we housed flies in custom optogenetic rigs with programmable, interchangeable LED bars, accomodating different channel rhodopsins and allowing us to either activate\cite{klapoetke2014} or inhibit\cite{mohammad2017} targeted neuron populations. Flies were otherwise aged using standard husbandry conditions.\cite{linford2013} Unless otherwise noted, in all treatment paradigms flies were exposed to light-activation (40 Hz, 32 duty cycle) only during ZT 0-12 of a 12:12 light:dark cycle, beginning at 3 days post-eclosion and continuing through adulthood, promoting a normal circadian rhythm and restricting neuronal manipulations to periods when flies would typically be alert and interacting with their environment ({fig:lifespanfig}A).

We focused our investigation on R2 and R4, specifically R4d, neurons, as these are required for the perception of dead conspecifics to modulate lifespan\cite{chakraborty2019} and for food perception to enhance starvation resistance\cite{luo2021}. When flies expressing CsChrimson\cite{klapoetke2014} in R2 neurons (R2>Chr) were exposed to red light during subjective day, we observed a significantly extended lifespan in both males and females compared to siblings aged in the dark. This effect was not observed in genotype control flies that did not express CsChrimson, ruling out general effects of red light on lifespan and indicating a direct effect of neuronal activation ({fig:lifespanfig}B). Activation of R4d neurons in a similar manner had no effect on lifespan [Supplemental]. However, when these neurons were silenced by exposing flies expressing the anoin channelrhodpsin GtACR1\cite{mohammad2017} to green light during subjective day, lifespan was significantly extended lifespan in males and females (\figref{fig:lifespanfig}C). These results should be interpreted with caution, because we observed that exposure to high-intensity green light caused a roughly 50\% (females) and 33\% (males) reduction in lifespan in all cohorts. Nevertheless, GtACR1 flies lived modestly but significantly longer than control flies in the light, and this difference was not observed in the dark.

Our results showing that R2 activation increased lifespan while R4d activation had no lifespan effect were somewhat unexpected, given that measures of R2 and R4 neuronal activity were observed to be higher after flies were exposed to dead conspecifics, which is known to accelerate aging and reduce lifespan in wild-type flies.\cite{chakraborty2019} We therefore decided to examine the effect of simultaneous activation of both R2 and R4 neurons using a split-GAL4 driver that targeted neurons in both populations. To our surprise, simultaneously activating both populations extended lifespan in males and females (\figref{fig:lifespanfig}D) to a \hl{greater} extent than R2 activation alone. While we observed that red light exposure during subjective day increased lifespan of CsChrimson-expressing flies as well as their genotypic controls in this experiment, the increase in lifespan was markedly greater in CsChrimson flies. 

Given the unexpected increase in lifespan observed following simultaneous R2/R4 activation, we asked whether manipulating the activity of the broader ellipsoid body would recapitulate or even potentiate the lifespan extension that we observed following R2 and R2/R4 neuron activation. We observed that inhibition of the EB had no effect on lifespan, suggesting that reduced activity has no effect on aging in normal conditions. Simultaneous activation of \hl{most} EB neurons increased lifespan to an equal or lesser extent than R2 or R/R4 activation alone, indicating that R2 and R4 are the primary lifespan-modulating ring neurons and/or that the net contribution of increased activity in other ring neuron populations is to modestly shorten, rather than extend, lifespan.

Together, these data demonstrate that R2 and R4 neurons can be recruited to positively influence lifespan. Strikingly, manipulating even small sets of neurons—in some cases, fewer than 10 per hemisphere\cite{omoto2018}—is sufficient to extend lifespan. Rather than merely gating or relaying lifespan-altering inputs generated upstream, the ellipsoid body appears to be an instructive generator of lifespan-modulating signals. Our results are consistent with known activity patterns of R2/R4 in other contexts. Ishomoto et al. reported that R2, R4m, and R4d neurons work together in an incoherent type 1 feedforward loop (I1-FFL).\cite{ishimoto2020} Upon simultaneous activation of R2/R4m and R4d, R4d signaling initially increased, but sustained activity of GABAergic R2 and R4m neurons gradually suppressed R4d activity. If optogenetic activation recruits R2/R4 in this feedforward signaling pattern, it may be that R2 activation, R2/R4 activation, and R4d inhibition are effectively the same intervention. Alternatively, they may engage partially or fully distinct mechanisms that converge on extended lifespan via different downstream mechanisms.

\subsection*{Different ring neuron manipulations produce distinct transcriptional profiles}

To investigate how R2 and R4 neurons modulate aging and whether they do so via similar or distinct mechanisms, we performed bulk RNA-sequencing on head tissues of flies after one week of R2 activation, R4d inhibition, or R2/R4 activation, under the same conditions used in the lifespan assays described above. We chose to sample flies after one week of optogenetic manipulation, to avoid survivorship bias that might arise at later time points while still allowing time for physiological differences to manifest. Flies were transferred directly from experimental rigs to liquid nitrogen, then decapitated by vortexing, and heads were collected with a sieve. Five biological replicates of 10 heads were collected for CsChrimson and control genotypes from flies maintained either in constant darkness or channel-activating red/green lights during the 12 hours of subjective day. Whole head samples comprised primarily brain tissue, although other tissues were present, including the head fat body and chitinous exoskeleton tissue. 

We used the DESeq2 package in R \cite{love2014} to first identify transcriptional changes associated with R2 neural activity. To accomplish this, we specified a model in which genotype (CsChrimson/GtACR1, control) and light stimulation (yes, no) served as main effects. A genotype x light interaction term represented changes specific to light-driven channelrhodopsin stimulation, and these were of primary interest to our analysis. One week after activation of R2 neurons, we identified 123 genes (70 up-regulated/53 down-regulated) whose expression values exhibited experiment-wise interaction p-values less than our cutoff of p < 0.01. Sample-based clustering using expression data from these genes produced clear separation, with the five replicates from each treatment forming four distinct groups [Fig xxx]. Hierarchical clustering revealed that samples from flies with activated R2 neurons formed a distinct group and that samples from CsChrimson flies maintained in constant darkness were more similar to samples from the control genotype than either was to the R2 activated treatment. Despite a stringent threshold of p < 0.01, we observed that one week of R2 neuron activation drove broad but pathway-enriched changes in the head. GO analysis of upregulated genes revealed enrichment for ribosome biogenesis and rRNA processing, insulin signaling, and response to starvation. Notably, these processes are not generally upregulated in tandem; decreases in IIS signaling have been shown to correspond to reduced translation in \textit{Drosophila}.\cite{essers2016} This suggests that direct R2 activation uncouples or differentially engages these two processes. Downregulated genes were enriched for protein folding and ER stress response pathways. Together, these results suggest that activation of R2 neurons induces a distinct \hl{metabolic state} in the head, characterized by altered nutrient signaling and reduced ER stress. It also caught our attention that many of the differentially expressed genes have been implicated in aging biology, with several known to modulate aging directly. For example, we observed the upregulation of multiple components of insulin-like signaling (e.g., \textit{foxo}, \textit{Thor}, \textit{InR}, \textit{dilp6}); upregulation of autophagy-related genes such as \textit{Atg101}; upregulation of genes involved in lipid metabolism, such as \textit{ImpL2}, which has also been linked to IIS-mediated lifespan extension [Partridge]; and higher levels of \textit{Cbs}, which encodes the rate-limiting enzyme in the transsulferation pathway and increases lifespan \cite{kabil2011}. Additional evidence for differentially expressed aging-related genes were identified at a significance cutoff of p < 0.05. These included upregulation of juvenile hormone esterase (\textit{Jhe}) and downregulation of genes involved in ER stress response, including UPR genes such as \textit{Hsc70}. Together, these results indicate that short-term, part-time activation (12L/12D) of a few dozen R2 neurons drives significant changes in multiple aging-, \hl{metabolic-}, and stress-response pathways.

Following one week of R4d silencing, far fewer genes were found to be differentially expressed with a signficance p < 0.01, although a significant effect green light may have masked some effects. As for the R2 data, sample-based clustering grouped biological replicates each as more closely related to each other than samples from all other treatments, demonstrating reproducibility. Interestingly, in all treatments green light drove strong upregulation of multiple genes that encode heat shock proteins (e.g., \textit{Hsp23}, \textit{Hsp26}, \textit{Hsp27}, \textit{Hsp70Bb}), which was significantly muted in flies with R4d neurons silenced. Other differentially expressed genes in this group include the enzyme tyrosine hydroxylase (\textit{ple}), which catelyzes the rate-determining step in dopamine synthesis, the serine protease \textit{scarface}, and several genes of unknown function. 

Temperatures in our ventilated and cooled optogenetic lifespan apparatuses were not significantly higher than those experienced by flies aged in constant darkness, indicating that something other than heat was the primary cause of the effects of green light on life span and gene expression. Fly vision is more sensitive to green than to red light,\cite{salcedo1999} and the short lifespan (Fig 1) and over-representation of upregulated heatshock proteins suggest that it represents a potent stressor at the intensity required for deep brain penetration. If so, it may be that a muted molecular response to moderate long-term stress contributed the extended lifespan of flies with silenced R4d neurons. It is, however, unknown whether similar mechanisms influence lifespan in more benign conditions.

We next asked how simultaneous activation of R2 and R4 compared to manipulations of the individual neuron populations. When R2 and R4 neurons were activated simultaneously, we once again observed excellent reproducibility with replicate samples within treatments being clustered together and flies with R2/R4 activated forming a cluster distinct from both genotype and light controls. At a cutoff of p < 0.01, R2/R4 activation resulted in roughly twice as many transcriptional changes as did R2 activation alone (280 DEGs, 198 up/82 down). GO analysis of upregulated genes was dominated by metabolic pathways related to amino acid metabolism and pentose phosphate and NADPH-generating processes. We also observed an upregulation of known lifespan-modulating genes involved in one carbon metabolism (\textit{Cbs}, \textit{Gnmt}, \textit{Glyp}, \textit{Glys}, \textit{Nmdmc}, \textit{Zw}).  In contrast to R2 activation alone, genes involved in starvation responses and lipid and carbohydrate catabolism were downregulated. Notably, rather than recapitulating either of the above two experiments, or containing their hits as a subset of R2/R4>Chr DEGs, the transcriptional profile of R2/R4-activated flies was distinct from those of R2 activation and R4d inhibition, even where it impinged on similar pathways.

\subsubsection*{Locomotor activity does not account for altered TAG and starvation phenotypes}

Our results suggest that R2 and R2/R4 activation alters energy balance or substrate preference in a way that is not fully captured by food intake or fat stores. Increased starvation sensitivity in females could reflect an inability to properly mobilize energy stores when food is actually scarce. Males, despite reduced TAG under both R2 and R2/R4 activation, maintained or improved starvation resistance, suggesting compensatory mechanisms such as a bias toward alternative energy stores. Decreased TAG levels or impaired starvation resistance could also be explained by increased energy expenditure. To test the latter possibility, we next asked whether neuronal activation alters locomotor activity. 

We used the \textit{Drosophila} Activity Monitoring System (“DAMS”)\cite{pfeiffenberger2010} to assay activity over a 48-hour period. In our optogenetic DAMS setup, flies were individually placed in horizontal tubes and stimulated by overhead LEDs. For the duration of the assay, flies were either given a 12-hour subjective day \hl{(V Hz etc)} or kept in darkness. We observed no difference in mean beam crossings for female R2-activated over 48 hours (\figref{fig:damsfig}A, Light x Genotype = 0.93), but we did see a modest decrease in male activity (Light x Genotype = 0.030). [Missing 1 week and 1 month.] [Missing naive R2/R4 flies.] After one week of pre-treatment, both male and female R2/R4-activated flies made fewer average beam crossings over a 48-hour period (p = 0.0052 and p < 0.001, respectively) than their dark controls. We detected a significant interaction effect for females (Light x Genotype p = 0.035), but not males (p = 0.47). Light-induced reductions in movement persisted for R2/R4-activated flies after one month of pre-treatment for both sexes (p = 0.043 for females, p = 0.010 for males), although there was no interaction effect for either sex at this time point (p = 0.079 and p = 0.12 for females and males, respectively). 

Since R2>Chr flies showed no differences in either food consumed (input) or TAG storage, we did not expect differences in activity (output) to account for the impaired starvation resistance of females, and these results aligned with our prediction. Despite their reduced TAG levels, R2>Chr males moved less than controls; thus, increased activity cannot explain their reduced TAG stores. Reduced movement may instead be a means of compensating for reduced or impaired energy storage. Notably, in agreement with our feeding data \hl{(fig)}, neither sex exhibited starvation-induced hyperactivity that accompanies chronic hunger in flies\hl{(cite)}. Direct R2 activation may therefore circumvent pathways that translate scarcity signals into food-seeking behaviors. 

R2/R4-activated flies of both sexes had depleted TAG stores compared to controls (\figref{fig:conexfig}D), despite no differences in feeding (\figref{fig:conexfig}B), and we hypothesized that this might reflect increased activity. Instead, we saw a modest reduction in movement over a 2-day period compared to controls, although activated flies appeared to adapt over time. 

Taken together, our data do not support increased gross locomotor activity as an explanation for decreased TAG levels or impaired starvation resistance.

\section{Discussion}
The central finding of this study is that direct manipulation of ellipsoid body (EB) ring neurons is sufficient to extend lifespan in \textit{Drosophila melanogaster}, establishing the EB as an instructive generator of lifespan-modulating signals rather than merely a passive conduit for sensory information. Previous work demonstrated that R2 and R4 neurons are required for the lifespan-shortening effects of death perception, wherein cohousing flies with dead conspecifics decreases fat stores, reduces starvation resistance, and accelerates aging through serotonin receptor 5-HT2A signaling {Chakraborty, 2019 #25}{Gendron, 2023 #128}. Our optogenetic experiments reveal that these same neuronal populations can be recruited to positively influence lifespan when activated or inhibited in specific patterns. Activation of R2 neurons, simultaneous activation of R2/R4 neurons, and inhibition of R4d neurons all extended lifespan, demonstrating that the EB functions as a bidirectional rheostat for aging. This finding aligns with the emerging view that sensory perception modulates aging through central neural circuits that integrate environmental information with internal physiological states {Pontillo, 2025 #233}. Strikingly, manipulating even small sets of neurons—in some cases, fewer than ten per hemisphere—is sufficient to alter lifespan, underscoring the remarkable sensitivity of aging processes to neural circuit activity.

The transcriptional signatures induced by different ring neuron manipulations provide mechanistic insight into how the EB may influence aging. Activation of R2 neurons upregulated components of insulin-like signaling (IIS), including \textit{foxo}, \textit{Thor}, \textit{InR}, and \textit{dilp6}, as well as autophagy-related genes and genes involved in one-carbon metabolism such as \textit{Cbs}, which encodes the rate-limiting enzyme in the transsulfuration pathway. The transsulfuration pathway has been established as a positive regulator of lifespan in \textit{Drosophila}, and its upregulation is both necessary and sufficient for dietary restriction-mediated longevity extension {Kabil, 2011 #170}{Shaposhnikov, 2018 #228}. The concurrent upregulation of IIS components and ribosome biogenesis genes is notable, as these processes are typically inversely regulated; reduced IIS signaling generally corresponds to decreased translation {Gao, 2023 #286}. This suggests that direct R2 activation may uncouple or differentially engage these pathways, potentially establishing a unique metabolic state that promotes longevity. The downregulation of ER stress response and protein folding genes further supports a model in which R2 activation reduces cellular stress, consistent with the known role of proteostasis in aging {Lim, 2022 #247}{Lim, 2024 #109}. Importantly, the transcription factor \textit{Foxo}, which was upregulated in R2-activated heads, is required in R2/R4 neurons for death perception to modulate lifespan {Gendron, 2023 #128}, suggesting that our optogenetic manipulations engage endogenous aging-regulatory pathways.

Different ring neuron manipulations produce distinct transcriptional profiles despite converging on extended lifespan. R2 activation induced a starvation-like transcriptional signature, while R2/R4 co-activation drove biosynthetic processes including amino acid metabolism and glycogen synthesis. R4d inhibition produced a more limited transcriptional response, though it notably attenuated the stress-induced upregulation of heat shock proteins observed in control flies exposed to green light. These divergent molecular signatures were not explained by changes in feeding behavior, fat storage, locomotor activity, or climbing ability, suggesting that the pro-longevity effects of these manipulations are not secondary to gross behavioral or physiological changes. This decoupling of transcriptional state from behavioral output is reminiscent of findings in dietary restriction paradigms, where the perception of food abundance can modulate lifespan independently of actual nutrient intake {Zhang, 2021 #127}{Lyu, 2021 #244}. Our data support a model in which EB ring neurons generate internal states that directly influence aging-related gene expression programs, potentially through downstream signaling to insulin-producing cells (IPCs) and other neuroendocrine centers. Indeed, prior work has shown that R2/R4 neuron activity regulates \textit{dilp3} and \textit{dilp5} expression in IPCs {Gendron, 2023 #128}, providing a plausible mechanism for how EB activity could be transduced into systemic physiological changes.

Valence experiments revealed an unexpected complexity in how EB circuit states relate may influence neural states. Activation of R2 neurons alone produced a robust negative valence in females, causing rapid disengagement from food sources and conditioned place aversion. In contrast, simultaneous activation of R2 and R4 neurons produced a strong positive valence that generalized across feeding-dependent and feeding-independent assays. This nonlinear relationship between circuit composition and behavioral output suggests that EB ring neurons do not make fixed, additive contributions to motivational state, but instead generate qualitatively distinct internal states through combinatorial recruitment. The central complex has been established as an integration center for sensory information and motor coordination {Kottler, 2019 #266}{Buhl, 2021 #22}, and our findings extend this role to include the generation of valence states that may influence aging. The observation that R2 activation is aversive yet extends lifespan challenges simple models in which positive affective states promote longevity. Instead, our data suggest that the relationship between valence and aging may be more nuanced, with different internal states engaging distinct downstream mechanisms that converge on lifespan regulation. This is consistent with evidence that neural states akin to fear, anxiety, and depression in \textit{Drosophila} have phenotypic manifestations and molecular causes similar to those observed in mammals {Chakraborty, 2019 #25}.

This research has implications for understanding how sensory perception modulates aging across taxa. Sensory inputs related to food, mates, and danger have been shown to influence lifespan in both \textit{Drosophila} and \textit{C. elegans} through conserved neuromodulatory systems {Pontillo, 2025 #233}{Chakraborty, 2019 #25}. The EB appears to function as a central node where these diverse sensory inputs are integrated and translated into physiological outputs that affect aging. The requirement for specific insulin-like peptides (\textit{dilp3} and \textit{dilp5} and the transcription factor Foxo in mediating death perception effects on lifespan {Gendron, 2023 #128} suggests that EB activity ultimately converges on conserved nutrient-sensing pathways. However, our transcriptomic data indicate that different EB activity patterns engage these pathways in distinct ways, potentially explaining how the same circuit can both accelerate aging (in response to death perception) and extend lifespan (when directly manipulated). This bidirectional capacity positions the EB as a potential target for interventions aimed at modulating aging, analogous to how dietary restriction engages nutrient-sensing pathways to promote longevity {Zhang, 2023 #4}{Kosakamoto, 2023 #185}. The conservation of central complex structure and function across insects, and the broader conservation of neuromodulatory systems that influence aging, suggest that insights from \textit{Drosophila} may inform our understanding of how neural states impact health and longevity in other species.

Our findings that discrete populations of ellipsoid body ring neurons can instructively modulate lifespan through the generation of distinct internal states carry potentially significant implications for understanding the neural basis of aging in humans. The \textit{Drosophila} central complex, of which the ellipsoid body is a core component, shares strong neuroanatomical and functional homology with the vertebrate basal ganglia {Gendron, 2023 #128}. Both structures serve as integrative hubs that process sensory information, coordinate motor output, and—critically—contribute to the generation of motivational and affective states. Recent work has expanded our understanding of the basal ganglia beyond its classical role in movement control, implicating this structure in the recognition of emotional stimuli, inference of others' emotional states, and awareness of subjective well-being {Gendron, 2023 #128}. Strikingly, neural states akin to fear, anxiety, and depression have been described in \textit{Drosophila}, with phenotypic and molecular components that are remarkably consistent with those observed in mammals {Chakraborty, 2019 #25}. The observation that manipulating EB ring neurons produces distinct valence states—aversive in the case of R2 activation alone, appetitive when R2 and R4 are co-activated—while both extend lifespan, suggests that the relationship between internal affective states and aging may be more nuanced than previously appreciated. If analogous circuits in the human basal ganglia similarly encode valence states that influence systemic physiology, this would provide a mechanistic framework for understanding associations between psychological states, chronic stress, and aging in human populations.
