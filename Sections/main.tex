\section{Results}

\subsection*{Manipulating subsets of EB neurons extends lifespan}

Ellipsoid body ring neurons R2 and R4d are required for shortened lifespan in response to death perception,\cite{chakraborty2019,gendron2023} but how they modulate lifespan is poorly understood. One possibility is that they gate sensory information that would otherwise shorten or extend lifespan. R2 and R4d neurons respond to visual stimuli\cite{seelig2013} and transmit visual information into the ellipsoid body.\cite{omoto2017} If their function is disrupted and the fly can no longer internally represent or “see” a salient visual stimulus, phenotypes normally induced by perceiving the stimulus are unlikely to manifest. A second, more intriguing possibility is that, rather than acting as a passive gate or conduit of lifespan-altering signals, the ellipsoid body is a prescriptive modulator of aging. In this case, we would predict that directly manipulating activity in one or more neuronal groups comprising the EB would be sufficient to alter lifespan. To distinguish between these possibilities, we performed a series of experiments in which we manipulated the activity of R2, R4d, and R2/R4 neurons in the EB using optogenetic approaches.  

In dissecting the ability of different ring neuron populations to modulate aging, several considerations informed our experimental design. First, prior investigations of EB ring neuron effects on lifespan have relied primarily on constitutive, life-long manipulations, and we sought to eliminate potential developmental effects and to restrict treatment to adults with a fully developed central complex. Second, we wanted to avoid tools that require introducing confounding factors, such as high temperatures, which can significantly affect lifespan and obscure treatment effects on aging.[cite] Third, we wanted the flexibility to perform acute, cyclic, or chronic manipulations in ethologically-relevant contexts that involve lifespan, behavioral, and health assays. To accommodate these requirements, we decided to adopt an optogenetic\cite{govorunova2015} approach, allowing us to 1) restrict treatment to adults with a fully developed central complex;\cite{lovick2017} 2) oberve freely-moving flies under normal laboratory conditions; and 3) easily apply different treatment paradigms by changing light-activation parameters and exposure times. To assay lifespans, we housed flies in custom optogenetic rigs with programmable, interchangeable LED bars, accomodating different channel rhodopsins and allowing us to either activate\cite{klapoetke2014} or inhibit\cite{mohammad2017} targeted neuron populations. Flies were otherwise aged using standard husbandry conditions.\cite{linford2013} Unless otherwise noted, in all treatment paradigms flies were exposed to light-activation (40 Hz, 32 duty cycle) only during ZT 0-12 of a 12:12 light:dark cycle, beginning at 3 days post-eclosion and continuing through adulthood, promoting a normal circadian rhythm and restricting neuronal manipulations to periods when flies would typically be alert and interacting with their environment ({fig:lifespanfig}A).

We focused our investigation on R2 and R4, specifically R4d, neurons, as these are required for the perception of dead conspecifics to modulate lifespan\cite{chakraborty2019} and for food perception to enhance starvation resistance\cite{luo2021}. When flies expressing CsChrimson\cite{klapoetke2014} in R2 neurons (R2>Chr) were exposed to red light during subjective day, we observed a significantly extended lifespan in both males and females compared to siblings aged in the dark. This effect was not observed in genotype control flies that did not express CsChrimson, ruling out general effects of red light on lifespan and indicating a direct effect of neuronal activation {fig:lifespanfig}B). Activation of R4d neurons in a similar manner had no effect on lifespan [Supplemental]. These results should be interpreted with caution, because we observed that exposure to high-intensity green light caused a roughly 50\% (females) and 33\% (males) reduction in lifespan in all cohorts. Nevertheless, GtACR1 flies lived modestly but significantly longer than control flies in the light, and this difference was not observed in the dark.

Our results showing that R2 activation increased lifespan while R4d activation had no lifespan effect were somewhat unexpected, given that measures of R2 and R4 neuronal activity were observed to be higher after flies were exposed to dead conspecifics, which is known to accelerate aging and reduce lifespan in wild-type flies.\cite{chakraborty2019} We therefore decided to examine the effect of simultaneous activation of both R2 and R4 neurons using a split-GAL4 driver that targeted neurons in both populations. \subsection*{Different ring neuron manipulations produce distinct transcriptional profiles}

To investigate how R2 and R4 neurons modulate aging and whether they do so via similar or distinct mechanisms, we performed bulk RNA-sequencing on head tissues of flies after one week of R2 activation, R4d inhibition, or R2/R4 activation, under the same conditions used in the lifespan assays described above. We chose to sample flies after one week of optogenetic manipulation to avoid survivorship bias that might arise at later time points \hl{this is good but also might consider why you waited a week to measure}. Flies were transferred directly from experimental rigs to liquid nitrogen, then decapitated by vortexing, and heads were collected with a sieve. Five biological replicates of 10 heads were collected for CsChrimson and control genotypes from flies maintained either in constant darkness or channel-activating red/green lights during the 12 hours of subjective day. Whole head samples comprised primarily brain tissue, although other tissues were present, including the head fat body and chitinous exoskeleton tissue. 

We used the DESeq2 package in R \cite{love2014} to first identify transcriptional changes associated with R2 neural activity. To accomplish this, we specified a model in which genotype (CsChrimson/GtACR1, control) and light stimulation (yes, no) served as main effects. A genotype x light interaction term represented changes specific to light-driven channelrhodopsin stimulation, and these were of primary interest to our analysis. One week after activation of R2 neurons, we identified 123 genes (70 up-regulated/53 down-regulated) whose expression values exhibited experiment-wise interaction p-values less than our cutoff of p < 0.01. Sample-based clustering using expression data from these genes produced clear separation, with the five replicates from each treatment forming four distinct groups [Fig xxx]. Hierarchical clustering revealed that samples from flies with activated R2 neurons formed a distinct group and that samples from CsChrimson flies maintained in constant darkness were more similar to samples from the control genotype than either was to the R2 activated treatment. Despite a stringent threshold of p < 0.01, we observed that one week of R2 neuron activation drove broad but pathway-enriched changes in the head. GO analysis of upregulated genes revealed enrichment for ribosome biogenesis and rRNA processing, insulin signaling, and response to starvation. Notably, these processes are not generally upregulated in tandem; decreases in IIS signaling have been shown to correspond to reduced translation in \textit{Drosophila}.\cite{essers2016} This suggests that direct R2 activation uncouples or differentially engages these two processes. Downregulated genes were enriched for protein folding and ER stress response pathways. Together, these results suggest that activation of R2 neurons induces a distinct \hl{metabolic state} in the head, characterized by altered nutrient signaling and reduced ER stress. It also caught our attention that many of the differentially expressed genes have been implicated in aging biology, with several known to modulate aging directly. For example, we observed the upregulation of multiple components of insulin-like signaling (e.g., \textit{foxo}, \textit{Thor}, \textit{InR}, \textit{dilp6}; upregulation of autophagy-related genes such as \textit{Atg101}; upregulation of genes involved in lipid metabolism, such as \textit{ImpL2}, which has also been linked to IIS-mediated lifespan extension [Partridge]; and higher levels of \textit{Cbs}, which encodes the rate-limiting enzyme in the transsulferation pathway and increases lifespan \hl{our pnas paper}. Additional evidence for differentially expressed aging-related genes were identified at a significance cutoff of p < 0.05. These included \hl{upregulation?} of juvenile hormone esterase (\textit{Jhe}) and downregulation of genes involved in ER stress response, including UPR genes such as \textit{Hsc70}. Together, these results indicate that short-term, part-time activation (12L/12D) of a few dozen R2 neurons drives significant changes in multiple aging-, \hl{metabolic-}, and stress-response pathways. 

Following one week of R4d silencing, far fewer genes were found to be differentially expressed with a signficance p < 0.01, although a significant effect green light may have masked some effects. As for the R2 data, sample-based clustering grouped biological replicates each as more closely related to each other than samples from all other treatments, demonstrating reproducibility. Only 11 genes (10 up-regulated/1 down-regulated) were associated with a significant p-value for the interaction term of the model, which indicates a differential response in flies with R4d neurons silenced. Interestingly, in all treatments green light drove strong upregulation of multiple genes that encode heat shock proteins (e.g., \textit{Hsp23}, \textit{Hsp26}, \textit{Hsp27}, \textit{Hsp70Bb}), which was significantly muted in flies with R4d neurons silenced. Temperatures in our ventilated and cooled optogenetic lifespan apparatuses were not significantly higher than those experienced by flies aged in constant darkness, indicating that something other than heat was the primary cause of the effects of green light on life span and gene expression. Fly vision is more sensitive to green than to red light,\cite{salcedo1999} and the short lifespan (Fig 1) and over-representation of upregulated heatshock proteins suggest that it represents a potent stressor at the intensity required for deep brain penetration. Because the above behavioral assays were conducted under uniform conditions, offering no competitive or decision-making element, they can tell us little about the internal states induced by each manipulation. In natural settings, ring neuron activity is responsive to stumuli that animals can act on--for example, by moving toward nutritive cues1 or away from threatening conditions.2 Therefore, we reasoned that the consequences of activating or inhibiting R2 and R4 neurons might only be visible in a context where flies can approach or avoid the associated internal state. 

Complementary behavioral assays allow us to infer whether activation or inhibition of neurons carries a positive or negative valence. We used the fly liquid-food interaction counter (FLIC) to ask whether flies would choose to modulate their own neurons, or whether they would avoid behaviors that induced modulation. We also placed flies in an arena in which one side was illuminated with red or green light and the other kept dark, observed which side they preferred, and noted their behavior when choosing the illuminated side.  

In the FLIC assay,3 a fly is placed in a chamber where she may choose to drink from either of two liquid wells (Fig XX), and interactions with the wells are recorded. The ceiling of each chamber contains optogenetic LEDs that can be programmed in open- or closed-loop configurations. We took advantage of the FLIC’s closed-loop capabilities to ask whether flies would differentially interact with wells that turned on these LEDs, giving flies control over their own optogenetic manipulation. In our FLIC setup, flies were presented with one neutral well (water) and one attractive well (10\% sucrose). When a “trigger fly” touched the sucrose well, red (for CsChrimson) or green (for GtACR1) LEDs simultaneously illuminated both her chamber and a neighboring chamber containing a control fly of the same genotype (“yoked control” in Fig XX). This design permitted the trigger fly to form an association between the sucrose well and the sensation of neuron activation (or inhibition), while the yoked control received random bursts of light from her perspective, allowing us to account for any non-associative behavioral changes (increased hunger, for example).

We can characterize several aspects of flies’ behavior in the FLIC to infer the internal state induced by the respective neuron manipulations. When a fly visits a well, it typically makes a series of closely-spaced contacts with the liquid (touches or licks). Each contact is recorded as an “Interaction” (Fig XX-Y). FLIC also lets us examine how many times a fly visits a well (“Events”, SuppXX) and how long these visits last (“Median Duration,” SuppXX). If trigger flies touched or drank from the S10 well more than controls did, visited it more frequently, or interacted with it for longer durations, we inferred that the manipulation produced a positively valenced internal state. Conversely, if flies quickly abandoned the trigger well or learned to avoid it, we concluded that the manipulation produced a negatively valenced internal state. Interactions remained suppressed even after a week (p = 0.035, Fig XX, middle) or a month (p < 0.01, Fig XX, right) of pre-treatment, indicating that 1) flies do not adapt to the chronic negatively-valenced neural state induced by R2 activation, or 2) the effect is transient and is lost during the periods in between feeding from the trigger well. Interestingly, despite the decrease in feeding time, R2>Chr flies made the same number of visits to the sucrose well as yoked controls (p = 0.11). This might indicate that the induced aversion is short-lived and flies are not conditioned to avoid the well, or simply that the flies remain hungry after repeatedly fleeing from the well. 

As discussed above, R2 activation induces a transcriptional profile in the head that suggests an internal “starvation” state, which could explain the negative valence of R2 activation in otherwise healthy flies. If R2>Chr trigger flies do feel hungry, their intense aversion to the nutritive sucrose well could be masking a hunger phenotype, and their short feeding durations could preclude induction of a food-seeking phenotype in R2>Chr yoked controls. To test this, we conducted a modified version of the trigger fly/yoked control setup in which each trigger fly had two yoked controls, one R2>Chr and one R2>WCS. As before, when a trigger fly interacted with the S10 well, its yoked controls received light in their chambers at the same time. In this modified setup, only genetic controls were used as trigger flies so that yoked controls would be exposed to more light at a frequency consistent with normal feeding patterns. If activation made R2>Chr flies feel hungry, we would expect the yoked R2>Chr flies, who formed no negative association with the sucrose well, to drink more than genetic yoked controls receiving equivalent bursts of light. However, we found no difference in sucrose licks between yoked control genotypes (p = XX), ruling out a masked hunger phenotype.

We also attempted to assess the valence of R4d inhibition, but these experiments were complicated by the green light itself. Green light is visible to flies and more intense than red light, and our flies appear to have found the sudden bursts of green light startling. R4d-inhibited flies showed an aversion to the trigger well, but so did their genetic controls, which manifested as decreased interactions and event durations for each (Supplement). Like R2>Chr flies, R4d>GtACR1 trigger flies visited the well as often as their yoked controls, despite this aversion. Since both genotypes responded to the light, we could not distinguish among 1) no valence or behavioral effect of R4d inhibition, 2) a positive valence masked by the startle effect, or 3) a negative valence that happens to resemble the observed behavior. New optogenetic tools could make future inhibitory FLIC experiments more reliable for measuring effects of inhibition.4

Given the limitations of studying R4d inhibition in the FLIC, we turned to R4d activation, reasoning that this could still provide information about internal states and behaviors governed by R4d activity. For light-naïve flies, we found that R4d activation, like R2 activation, decreased interactions with the S10 well (p < 0.01), and decreased the durations of visits (p < 0.01), without decreasing the number of visits. Reduced interactions persisted after a week of pre-treatment (p < 0.01); however, after a month of light exposure, flies appeared to acclimate, as no decrease in licks was observed. 

Since R2 and R4d activation each induced aversive responses, we next asked whether simultaneous activation of R2 and R4 would produce a similar aversion, an additive aversion, or an entirely different behavioral outcome. Strikingly, the results were qualitatively different than we observed for R2 or R4d alone. R2/R4 activation significantly increased interactions with S10 (p < 0.01, Fig XX, left). R2/R4-activated flies also visited the S10 well more frequently than controls (p < 0.01, SupXX), suggesting that activation reinforced engagement with S10. Together, these data indicate a clear preference for the sucrose trigger well. Once again, the difference in interactions persists after a week (p < 0.01, XX, middle) or a month (p < 0.01, Fig XX, right) of pre-treatment with red light. Interestingly, median duration of visits did not change. This pattern—approaching more often and interacting more vigorously, but staying for only a typical duration—may reflect increased ‘wanting’ of the stimulus, rather than increased ‘liking’ [cite Kristy and/or Rachel]. Alternatively, satiety may override the drive to interact with the sucrose well, causing the fly to depart after some threshold is reached.

\subsection*{Valence of R4d activation is uniquely context-dependent}

To ask whether the valences observed in FLIC were context-dependent or generalizable, we assayed place preferences using a rectangular 12-lane arena with programmable LEDs mounted under a transparent floor \figref{fig:arenafig}. We loaded one fly per lane, alternating between control and experimental genotypes. Light conditions matched FLIC parameters (48 Hz, 32 dc, 18 V), but this time, an open-loop program was used, with one half of the floor illuminated by red or green light and the other half left unilluminated. Flies were given a 10-minute acclimation period with no lights (“Pre-Treatment”); then a 60 min period in which, on the lit side, LEDs cycled on and off in 5-second intervals (“Treatment”); and finally, a 10-minute cooldown period with no lights (“Recovery”). Preference index (PI) was used to quantify arena side bias, with positive values indicating a preference for the lit side. 

Consistent with our observations in the FLIC, R2>Chr flies demonstrated an aversion to the lit side of the arena. All flies exhibited an initial right-side bias during the dark pre-treatment acclimation period. During the treatment phase, R2>Chr female preference shifted to the dark (left) side of the arena (PI = XX), while controls showed an unchanged preference for the right (now, lit) side (PI = XX). R2>Chr left-side preference persisted for at least 10 minutes after the light treatment ended (PI = XX), suggesting a conditioned place preference driven by an aversion to R2 activation. This aversion is robust to different light regimes, as we see a similar pattern at 200 Hz (Supp XX). These results, combined with our FLIC data, demonstrate that the negative valence of R2 activation holds across contexts for female flies. Notably, the persistent place avoidance into the dark recovery period contrasts with what we observed in FLIC, where flies quickly abandoned the trigger well but still approached it with typical frequency (Fig XX). This suggests that contextual features of different assays, like food perception, environmental structure, or cumulative activation, may modulate the character or expression of negative valence induced by R2 activation.

Interestingly, male flies exhibited marked a preference for the lit side of the arena during both treatment and recovery at 48 Hz (Fig XX), although this was less pronounced at 200 Hz (Supp XX). While we currently lack transcriptomic profiles of R2-activated males, we speculate that the same signal may encode different valences for males and females due to their divergent life histories; for example, the detection of females may be welcome to males, but may drive mated females to seek oviposition sites elsewhere. 

Our study of R4d inhibition in the FLIC was confounded by the startling effect of sudden visible light in a closed-loop paradigm, but the open-loop paradigm of the arena alleviated this concern. Both control and GtACR1 females shifted in preference toward the lit side of the arena during treatment; by contrast, control and GtACR1 male preference shifted markedly toward the unlit side. This could reflect 1) no valence, but a sex-specific reaction to light itself; or 2) positive and negative valence, respectively, masked by coincidentally similar control behavior. Unlike red-light experiments, inhibitory GtACR1 experiments are bounded in intensity by physiological considerations (principally excessive heat). Going forward, the use of recently developed red-gated inhibitory channels will help us probe behaviors under a broader range of light regimes. 

R4d>Chr flies, like R2>Chr flies, showed an aversion to activation in the FLIC. In the arena, however, R4d>Chr flies showed a bias toward the lit side comparable to their pre-treatment behavior and to the preference of genetic controls (Fig XX) at 48 Hz. At 200 Hz, female preference shifted toward the unlit side of the arena during treatment and recovery (Supp XX). By contrast, males strongly preferred the unlit side at 48 Hz (Fig XX), but this preference was attenuated at 200 Hz (Supp XX). Thus, in contrast to what we observed when activating R2, the valence of R4d activation was sensitive to light intensity in the arena. R4d neurons may therefore encode more context-dependent signals than R2 neurons and perhaps play a more secondary or modulatory role in establishing valence. 

Given that R4d activation produced different results in the arena than in the FLIC, we wondered whether the strong positive valence of R2/R4 activation would hold across contexts, like R2 activation, or whether it would track R4d’s context dependence. When simultaneously activating R2 and R4 neurons at 48 Hz, we observed a strong preference for the lit side of the arena in both females (Fig XX) and males (Fig XX) that persisted during the recovery phase. This strong light preference was also observed at 200 Hz for both sexes (Supp XX). The positive valence of simultaneous R2/R4 activation was therefore robust across different light conditions and behavioral assays, producing behaviors distinct from activation of R2 and R4d alone. While the R2/R4 split-GAL4 includes a sub-population not targeted in our individual manipulations, R4m, the switch from aversion to attraction in both assays suggests that co-activation of R2 and R4 neurons generates a unique motivational state, rather than summing their individual contributions.
