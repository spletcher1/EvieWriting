\section{Results}

\subsection*{Manipulating subsets of EB neurons extends lifespan}

Ellipsoid body ring neurons R2 and R4d are required for shortened lifespan in response to death perception,\cite{chakraborty2019,gendron2023} but how they modulate lifespan is poorly understood. One possibility is that they gate sensory information that would otherwise shorten or extend lifespan. R2 and R4d neurons respond to visual stimuli\cite{seelig2013} and transmit visual information into the ellipsoid body.\cite{omoto2017} If their function is disrupted and the fly can no longer internally represent or “see” a salient visual stimulus, phenotypes normally induced by perceiving the stimulus are unlikely to manifest. A second, more intriguing possibility is that, rather than acting as a passive gate or conduit of lifespan-altering signals, the ellipsoid body is a prescriptive modulator of aging. In this case, we would predict that directly manipulating activity in one or more neuronal groups comprising the EB would be sufficient to alter lifespan. To distinguish between these possibilities, we performed a series of experiments in which we manipulated the activity of R2, R4d, and R2/R4 neurons in the EB using optogenetic approaches.

In dissecting the ability of different ring neuron populations to modulate aging, several considerations informed our experimental design. First, prior investigations of EB ring neuron effects on lifespan have relied primarily on constitutive, life-long manipulations,\cite{gendron2023}, and we sought to eliminate potential developmental effects and to restrict treatment to adults with a developed central complex.\cite{lovick2017} Second, we wanted to avoid tools that require introducing confounding factors, such as high temperatures, which can significantly affect lifespan and obscure treatment effects on aging.[\hl{cite}] Third, we wanted the flexibility to perform acute, cyclic, or chronic manipulations in ethologically-relevant contexts that involve lifespan, behavior, and health assays. To accommodate these requirements, we decided to adopt an optogenetic\cite{govorunova2015} approach, which allowed us to easily apply different treatment paradigms to freely-moving and fully developed adults by changing light-activation parameters and exposure times. To assay lifespans, we housed flies in custom optogenetic rigs with programmable, interchangeable LED bars, accomodating different channel rhodopsins and allowing us to either activate\cite{klapoetke2014} or inhibit\cite{mohammad2017} targeted neuron populations. Flies were otherwise aged using standard husbandry conditions \cite{linford2013}. Unless otherwise noted, in all treatment paradigms flies were exposed to light-activation (40 Hz, 32 duty cycle) only during ZT 0-12 (i.e., 8am - 8pm) of a 12:12 light:dark cycle, beginning at 3 days post-eclosion and continuing through adulthood, promoting a normal circadian rhythm and restricting neuronal manipulations to periods when flies would typically be alert and interacting with their environment ({fig:lifespanfig}A).

We focused our investigation on R2 and R4, specifically R4d, neurons, as these are required for the perception of dead conspecifics to modulate lifespan\cite{gendron2023} and for food perception to enhance starvation resistance\cite{luo2021}. When flies expressing CsChrimson\cite{klapoetke2014} in R2 neurons (R2>Chr \hl{this abbreviation is here but no used again. This effect was not observed in genotype control flies that did not express CsChrimson, ruling out general effects of red light on lifespan and indicating a direct effect of neuronal activation. Activation of R4d neurons in a similar manner had no effect on lifespan (\figref{fig:lifespansupp}A). However, when these neurons were silenced by exposing flies expressing the anion channelrhodpsin GtACR1\cite{mohammad2017} to green light during subjective day, lifespan was significantly extended lifespan in females and males (\figref{fig:lifespanfig}C). These results should be interpreted with caution, however, because we observed that exposure to high-intensity green light caused a significant reduction in lifespan in all cohorts (roughly 50\% in females and 33\% in males). Nevertheless, GtACR1 flies lived modestly but significantly longer than control flies in the light, and this difference was not observed in the dark.

Our results showing that R2 activation increased lifespan while R4d activation had no lifespan effect were somewhat unexpected, given that measures of R2 and R4 neuronal activity were observed to be higher after flies were exposed to dead conspecifics,\cite{gendron2023}, which is known to accelerate aging and reduce lifespan in wild-type flies \cite{chakraborty2019}. We therefore decided to examine the effect of simultaneous activation of R2 and R4 (comprising R4d and R4m sub-populations) using a split-GAL4 driver. To our surprise, simultaneously activating both populations extended lifespan in males and females (\figref{fig:lifespanfig}D) to a \hl{greater} extent than did R2 activation alone. In this experiment, red light exposure \hl{dont have to say subjective day cause we defined conditions above} modestly increased lifespan of CsChrimson-expressing flies as well as their genotypic controls, but the increase in lifespan was markedly greater in CsChrimson flies. 

Given the substantial increase in lifespan observed following simultaneous R2/R4 activation, we asked whether even broader manipulation of ellipsoid body neurons would recapitulate or even potentiate the lifespan extension that we observed following R2 and R2/R4 neuron activation. Using a driver that targets additional EB neuron populations, including at least R3 neurons, we observed that inhibition no effect on lifespan (\figref{fig:lifespanfig}E, left) and activation increased lifespan to an equal or lesser extent than R2 or R/R4 activation alone (\figref{fig:lifespanfig}E, right). These data suggest that reduced activity in the EB more generally has no effect on aging in normal conditions and that enhanced EB neural activity promotes longevity primarily through R2 and R4 neurons.

Together, these data demonstrate that R2 and R4 neurons can be recruited to positively influence lifespan. Strikingly, manipulating even small sets of neurons—in some cases, fewer than 10 per hemisphere\cite{omoto2018}—is sufficient to extend lifespan. Rather than merely gating or relaying lifespan-altering inputs generated upstream, our data support the notiona that the ellipsoid body is an instructive generator of lifespan-modulating signals. These results are consistent with known activity patterns of R2/R4 in other contexts. Ishomoto et al. reported that R2, R4m, and R4d neurons work together in an incoherent type 1 feedforward loop (I1-FFL).\cite{ishimoto2020} Upon simultaneous activation of R2/R4m and R4d, R4d signaling initially increased, but sustained activity of GABAergic R2 and R4m neurons gradually suppressed R4d activity. If optogenetic activation recruits R2/R4 in this feedforward signaling pattern, it may be that R2 activation, R2/R4 activation, and R4d inhibition are effectively the same intervention. Alternatively, they may engage partially or fully distinct pathways that converge on extended lifespan via different downstream mechanisms.

\subsection*{Different ring neuron manipulations produce distinct transcriptional profiles}

To investigate how R2 and R4 neurons modulate aging and whether they do so via similar or distinct mechanisms, we performed bulk RNA-sequencing on head tissues of female flies after one week of R2 activation, R4d inhibition, or R2/R4 activation, under the same conditions used in the lifespan assays described above. We chose to sample flies after one week of optogenetic manipulation to avoid survivorship bias that might arise at later time points while still allowing time for physiological differences to manifest. Flies were transferred directly from experimental rigs to liquid nitrogen, then decapitated by vortexing, and heads were collected with a sieve. Five biological replicates of 10 heads were collected for CsChrimson and control genotypes from flies maintained either in constant darkness or channel-activating red/green lights during the 12 hours of subjective day. Whole head samples comprised primarily brain tissue, although other tissues were present, including the head fat body and chitinous exoskeleton tissue. 

We used the DESeq2\cite{love2014} package in R to first identify transcriptional changes associated with R2 neural activity. We specified a statistical model in which genotype (CsChrimson/GtACR1, control) and light stimulation (yes, no) served as main effects. A genotype x light interaction term was used to identify significant transcriptional changes that were specific to light-driven channelrhodopsin stimulation, and these were of primary interest to our analysis. One week after activation of R2 neurons, we identified 123 genes (70 up-regulated and 53 down-regulated) whose expression values exhibited experiment-wise interaction p-values less than our cutoff of p < 0.01. Expression data from these genes produced clear separation of samples by treatment, indicating strong reproducibiltiy across each of the five replicates (\figref{fig:seqfig}A). Hierarchical clustering revealed that samples from flies with activated R2 neurons (i.e., R2>CsChrimson flies exposed to red light) formed a distinct group from flies without neuronal activation, including flies expressing CsChrimson but maintained in constant darkness as well as samples from the control genotypes maintained in either darkeness or red light. Despite a stringent threshold of p < 0.01, we observed that one week of R2 neuron activation drove broad but pathway-enriched changes in the head. GO analysis of upregulated genes revealed enrichment for ribosome biogenesis and rRNA processing, insulin signaling, and response to starvation (\figref{fig:seqfig}B). Downregulated genes were enriched for protein folding and ER stress response pathways (\figref{fig:seqfig}C). Together, these results suggest that activation of R2 neurons induces a distinct \hl{metabolic state} in the head, characterized by altered nutrient signaling and reduced ER stress. It also caught our attention that many of the differentially expressed genes have been implicated in aging biology, with several known to modulate aging directly. For example, we observed the upregulation of multiple components of insulin-like signaling (e.g., \textit{foxo}, \textit{Thor}, \textit{InR}, \textit{dilp6}) [\hl{[cite these]}]; upregulation of autophagy-related genes such as \textit{Atg101} \hl{[cite]}; upregulation of genes involved in lipid metabolism, such as \textit{ImpL2}, which has also been linked to IIS-mediated lifespan extension [\hl{Partridge + fat stuff}]; and higher levels of \textit{Cbs}, which encodes the rate-limiting enzyme in the transsulferation pathway and increases lifespan \cite{kabil2011}. Additional evidence for differentially expressed aging-related genes was identified at a significance cutoff of p < 0.05. These included upregulation of juvenile hormone esterase (\textit{Jhe}) \hl{[cite]} and downregulation of genes involved in ER stress response, including UPR genes such as \textit{Hsc70} \hl{[cite]}. Together, these results indicate that short-term, part-time activation (12L/12D) of a few dozen R2 neurons drives significant changes in multiple aging-, \hl{metabolic-}, and stress-response pathways.

\subsection*{EB ring neuron manipulations uncouple nutrient-state signaling from behavioral and physiological outputs}
If R2 and R4 neurons necessarily worked together to transduce information--for example, via an I1-FFL circuit\cite{ishimoto2020}--we would expect R2 activation, R2/R4 activation, and R4d inhibition to produce similar downstream effects regardless of context. However, manipulations of R2 and R4d neurons alone and in combination produced markedly divergent transcriptional profiles in head tissues (\figref{fig:seqfig}H-I). Consistent with the ellipsoid body's role as in integrative center, it may be that activity patterns in these ring neuron populations reflect different interpretations of sensory stimuli or internal physiological signals, which direct distinct beahvioral and physiological responses. To discriminate among such responses and to better understand what information each manipulation imparts to flies, we characterized feeding behavior, energy management, and overall organismal performance following neuronal manipulations. 

\subsubsection*{Feeding behavior does not explain nutrient-state transcriptional signatures}

Reduced nutrient intake, as well as homeostatic hunger drive, slow aging in \textit{Drosophila},\cite{pletcher2002,weaver2023}, and GO analyses of differentially expressed genes in flies with activated R2 and R2/R4 neurons identified pathways indicative of altered nutrient state and metabolism. We therefore asked whether the effects of neuronal activation might include changes in feeding that indicate a hunger state or that underlie the observed transcriptional differences. To assay for feeding changes, we measured the volume of food intake using a consumption-excretion (“con-ex”) assay \cite{shell2018}. Flies were placed on food containing a blue dye tracer for 24 hours, after which we quantified the amount of dye deposited on vial walls.  Because flies can behaviorally and physiologically adapt to chronic neuron manipulation,\cite{weaver2023}, we tested light-naïve flies as well as flies that had been pre-treated with light for one week or one month, to evaluate changes over a significant portion of the lifespan.

Although R2 neurons are among the populations targeted by SS02769-GAL4, our GO analysis indicates that R2>Chr and R2/R4d>Chr flies entered markedly different internal states. Because nutrient-state-associated transcriptional changes following R2 and R2/R4d activation were not explained by altered feeding, we next asked whether these manipulations affected metabolic physiology more directly by measuring TAG stores and starvation resistance. After one week of R2 activation, TAG levels were unchanged in females but significantly reduced in males (\figref{fig:conexfig}C), whereas R2/R4 activation significantly reduced TAG in both sexes (\figref{fig:conexfig}D). \subsubsection*{Physiological effects of EB manipulatoin are not attributable to altered locomotor activity}

Because feeding behavior and TAG measurements did not fully explain the physiological consequences of R2 and R2/R4d activation, we next asked whether these manipulations altered gross locomotor activity. Increased movement could, in principle, contribute to reduced TAG stores or impaired starvation resistance, whereas unchanged or reduced movement would argue against this explanation. We therefore used the \textit{Drosophila} Activity Monitoring System (DAMS) \cite{pfeiffenberger2010} to measure activity over 48 hours during optogenetic stimulation. Flies were housed individually in horizontal tubes and maintained either under activating light or in darkness for the duration of the assay. Acute R2 activation did not alter mean beam crossings in females (\figref{fig:damsfig}A; Light $\times$ Genotype, p = 0.93), but modestly reduced activity in males (Light $\times$ Genotype, p = 0.030). After one week of pretreatment, R2/R4-activated flies of both sexes also exhibited reduced locomotor activity relative to dark controls  (p = 0.0052 and p < 0.0010 for males and females, respectively), with a significant interaction in females (p = 0.035) but not males (p = 0.47). Following one month of pretreatment, this reduction persisted in both sexes (p = 0.043 for females, p = 0.010 for males), although interaction effects were no longer significant (p = 0.079 and p = 0.12 for females and males, respectively). Thus, neuronal activation did not increase gross locomotor activity under any condition tested. This result is particularly informative in R2>Chr males and R2/R4>Chr flies, in which TAG stores were reduced despite unchanged feeding and lower, rather than higher, movement. Moreover, we did not observe the hyperactivity classically associated with starvation-driven food-seeking in flies, consistent with our earlier finding that these manipulations impose nutrient-state-like programs without evoking canonical behavioral responses. Together, these data argue that increased gross locomotor activity is unlikely to account for the changes in TAG levels or starvation resistance caused by EB neuron activation.

To better understand the distinct internal states imposed by lifespan-extending manipulations of EB ring neurons, we first examined their valence using the FLIC assay, which permits flies to associate optogenetic stimulation with self-initiated interactions at a feeding site.\cite{ro2014} In this paradigm, individual flies chose between a neutral water well and an attractive 10\% sucrose well, while closed-loop optogenetic stimulation (red light for CsChrimson or green light for GtACR1) was triggered selectively by contact with the sucrose well (Fig XX). Each “trigger” fly was paired with a yoked control that received identical light stimulation determined solely by the behavior of the trigger fly, without relationship to its own feeding behavior. This design allowed us to ask whether activation or inhibition of specific EB neuron populations altered the motivational value of the sucrose-associated well while accounting for non-associative consequences of light exposure (increased hunger, for example). As with our behavioral assays, we performed these experiments both in light-naïve flies and in flies pre-exposed to light for one week or one month to explore whether chronic manipulation might induce neuronal or behavioral/physiological compensation. Because FLIC captures several features of feeding behavior, it allowed us to assess the valence associated with each manipulation across multiple behavioral dimensions. Each contact with the liquid was scored as an “interaction,” and these data were further parsed into the number of discrete well visits (“events”) and the duration of those visits (“median duration”). Increased sucrose interactions, more frequent visits, or longer visit durations were interpreted as evidence of positive valence, whereas rapid abandonment of the trigger well or learned avoidance was taken to indicate negative valence. In summary, the valence experiments reveal that EB-dependent regulation of behavior is nonlinear and depends on both circuit composition and behavioral context. Across two complementary assays, activation of R2 alone consistently produced negative valence in females, whereas co-activation of R2/R4 produced a robust positive valence that generalized across feeding-dependent and feeding-independent paradigms and persisted after chronic light exposure, indicating that these effects reflect stable properties of EB circuit state rather than acute stimulation artifacts. In the FLIC, the R2/R4 phenotype was characterized by increased interactions and more frequent returns without prolonged visit duration, a pattern consistent with enhanced approach or incentive salience rather than simply increased consummatory behavior \textit{per se}. By contrast, the behavioral consequences of R4d manipulation were more context dependent, and the place-preference experiments further revealed that the valence associated with R2 activation can differ by sex, suggesting that the same circuit output may be interpreted differently depending on internal state or life-history context. Together, these findings support a model in which EB ring neurons do not make fixed, additive contributions to motivational state, but instead generate qualitatively distinct internal states through combinatorial recruitment. This raises the intriguing possibility that distinct EB activity states engage different downstream programs while nevertheless converging on lifespan regulation.

\section{Discussion}
The central finding of this study is that direct manipulation of ellipsoid body (EB) ring neurons is sufficient to extend lifespan in \textit{Drosophila melanogaster}, establishing the EB as an instructive generator of lifespan-modulating signals rather than merely a passive conduit for sensory information. Previous work demonstrated that R2 and R4 neurons are required for the lifespan-shortening effects of death perception, wherein cohousing flies with dead conspecifics decreases fat stores, reduces starvation resistance, and accelerates aging through serotonin receptor 5-HT2A signaling.\cite{chakraborty2019,gendron2023} Our optogenetic experiments reveal that these same neuronal populations can be recruited to positively influence lifespan when activated or inhibited in specific patterns. Activation of R2 neurons, simultaneous activation of R2/R4 neurons, and inhibition of R4d neurons all extended lifespan, demonstrating that the EB functions as a bidirectional rheostat for aging. This finding aligns with the emerging view that sensory perception modulates aging through central neural circuits that integrate environmental information with internal physiological states {Pontillo, 2025 233}. Strikingly, manipulating even small sets of neurons—in some cases, fewer than ten per hemisphere—is sufficient to alter lifespan, underscoring the remarkable sensitivity of aging processes to neural circuit activity.

The transcriptional signatures induced by different ring neuron manipulations provide mechanistic insight into how the EB may influence aging. Activation of R2 neurons upregulated components of insulin-like signaling (IIS), including \textit{foxo}, \textit{Thor}, \textit{InR}, and \textit{dilp6}, as well as autophagy-related genes and genes involved in one-carbon metabolism such as \textit{Cbs}, which encodes the rate-limiting enzyme in the transsulfuration pathway. The transsulfuration pathway has been established as a positive regulator of lifespan in \textit{Drosophila}, and its upregulation is both necessary and sufficient for dietary restriction-mediated longevity extension {Kabil, 2011 170}{Shaposhnikov, 2018 228}. The concurrent upregulation of IIS components and ribosome biogenesis genes is notable, as these processes are typically inversely regulated; reduced IIS signaling generally corresponds to decreased translation {Gao, 2023 286}. This suggests that direct R2 activation may uncouple or differentially engage these pathways, potentially establishing a unique metabolic state that promotes longevity. The downregulation of ER stress response and protein folding genes further supports a model in which R2 activation reduces cellular stress, consistent with the known role of proteostasis in aging {Lim, 2022 247}{Lim, 2024 109}. Importantly, the transcription factor \textit{Foxo}, which was upregulated in R2-activated heads, is required in R2/R4 neurons for death perception to modulate lifespan {Gendron, 2023 128}, suggesting that our optogenetic manipulations engage endogenous aging-regulatory pathways.

Different ring neuron manipulations produce distinct transcriptional profiles despite converging on extended lifespan. R2 activation induced a starvation-like transcriptional signature, while R2/R4 co-activation drove biosynthetic processes including amino acid metabolism and glycogen synthesis. R4d inhibition produced a more limited transcriptional response, though it notably attenuated the stress-induced upregulation of heat shock proteins observed in control flies exposed to green light. These divergent molecular signatures were not explained by changes in feeding behavior, fat storage, locomotor activity, or climbing ability, suggesting that the pro-longevity effects of these manipulations are not secondary to gross behavioral or physiological changes. This decoupling of transcriptional state from behavioral output is reminiscent of findings in dietary restriction paradigms, where the perception of food abundance can modulate lifespan independently of actual nutrient intake {Zhang, 2021 127}{Lyu, 2021 244}. Our data support a model in which EB ring neurons generate internal states that directly influence aging-related gene expression programs, potentially through downstream signaling to insulin-producing cells (IPCs) and other neuroendocrine centers. Indeed, prior work has shown that R2/R4 neuron activity regulates \textit{dilp3} and \textit{dilp5} expression in IPCs {Gendron, 2023 128}, providing a plausible mechanism for how EB activity could be transduced into systemic physiological changes.

Valence experiments revealed an unexpected complexity in how EB circuit states relate may influence neural states. Activation of R2 neurons alone produced a robust negative valence in females, causing rapid disengagement from food sources and conditioned place aversion. In contrast, simultaneous activation of R2 and R4 neurons produced a strong positive valence that generalized across feeding-dependent and feeding-independent assays. This nonlinear relationship between circuit composition and behavioral output suggests that EB ring neurons do not make fixed, additive contributions to motivational state, but instead generate qualitatively distinct internal states through combinatorial recruitment. The central complex has been established as an integration center for sensory information and motor coordination {Kottler, 2019 266}{Buhl, 2021 22}, and our findings extend this role to include the generation of valence states that may influence aging. The observation that R2 activation is aversive yet extends lifespan challenges simple models in which positive affective states promote longevity. Instead, our data suggest that the relationship between valence and aging may be more nuanced, with different internal states engaging distinct downstream mechanisms that converge on lifespan regulation. This is consistent with evidence that neural states akin to fear, anxiety, and depression in \textit{Drosophila} have phenotypic manifestations and molecular causes similar to those observed in mammals {Chakraborty, 2019 25}.

This research has implications for understanding how sensory perception modulates aging across taxa. Sensory inputs related to food, mates, and danger have been shown to influence lifespan in both \textit{Drosophila} and \textit{C. elegans} through conserved neuromodulatory systems {Pontillo, 2025 233}{Chakraborty, 2019 25}. The EB appears to function as a central node where these diverse sensory inputs are integrated and translated into physiological outputs that affect aging. The requirement for specific insulin-like peptides \textit{dilp3} and \textit{dilp5} and the transcription factor Foxo in mediating death perception effects on lifespan {Gendron, 2023 128} suggests that EB activity ultimately converges on conserved nutrient-sensing pathways. However, our transcriptomic data indicate that different EB activity patterns engage these pathways in distinct ways, potentially explaining how the same circuit can both accelerate aging (in response to death perception) and extend lifespan (when directly manipulated). This bidirectional capacity positions the EB as a potential target for interventions aimed at modulating aging, analogous to how dietary restriction engages nutrient-sensing pathways to promote longevity {Zhang, 2023 4}{Kosakamoto, 2023 185}. The conservation of central complex structure and function across insects, and the broader conservation of neuromodulatory systems that influence aging, suggest that insights from \textit{Drosophila} may inform our understanding of how neural states impact health and longevity in other species.

Our findings that discrete populations of ellipsoid body ring neurons can instructively modulate lifespan through the generation of distinct internal states carry potentially significant implications for understanding the neural basis of aging in humans. The \textit{Drosophila} central complex, of which the ellipsoid body is a core component, shares strong neuroanatomical and functional homology with the vertebrate basal ganglia {Gendron, 2023 128}. Both structures serve as integrative hubs that process sensory information, coordinate motor output, and—critically—contribute to the generation of motivational and affective states. Recent work has expanded our understanding of the basal ganglia beyond its classical role in movement control, implicating this structure in the recognition of emotional stimuli, inference of others' emotional states, and awareness of subjective well-being {Gendron, 2023 128}. Strikingly, neural states akin to fear, anxiety, and depression have been described in \textit{Drosophila}, with phenotypic and molecular components that are remarkably consistent with those observed in mammals {Chakraborty, 2019 25}. The observation that manipulating EB ring neurons produces distinct valence states—aversive in the case of R2 activation alone, appetitive when R2 and R4 are co-activated—while both extend lifespan, suggests that the relationship between internal affective states and aging may be more nuanced than previously appreciated. If analogous circuits in the human basal ganglia similarly encode valence states that influence systemic physiology, this would provide a mechanistic framework for understanding associations between psychological states, chronic stress, and aging in human populations.
